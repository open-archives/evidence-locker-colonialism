## Executive Summary

The Bretton Woods system, established in July 1944 by delegates from 44 nations at the Mount Washington Hotel in New Hampshire, created the architecture of the post-World War II global financial order. It forged two permanent institutions—the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the International Bank for Reconstruction and Development (the World Bank)—designed to ensure exchange rate stability, prevent competitive devaluations, and promote economic growth. This financial architecture effectively codified Pax Americana and granted the U.S. what would later be termed an "exorbitant privilege" in global affairs.

The system was built on a U.S. dollar pegged to gold at $35 per ounce, with all other currencies fixed to the dollar. This arrangement gave the U.S. currency a dominant position in the world economy, allowing the United States to run trade deficits without devaluing. The primary architects were British economist John Maynard Keynes, who proposed a global currency called the "bancor," and American Treasury official Harry Dexter White, whose more limited vision ultimately prevailed.

The system contained a fatal flaw. In 1960, economist Robert Triffin exposed a fundamental problem—if the United States stopped running balance of payments deficits, the world would lose its primary source of liquidity; if deficits continued, confidence in the dollar would erode. This "Triffin Dilemma" proved terminal. By 1971, U.S. gold reserves were hemorrhaging due to spending on the Vietnam War and domestic programs. President Richard Nixon closed the gold window on August 15, 1971—the "Nixon Shock"—effectively ending the Bretton Woods system.

The petrodollar system emerged in 1974 as its replacement. Henry Kissinger negotiated an agreement with Saudi Arabia: the U.S. would provide arms and protection in exchange for oil priced exclusively in dollars, with surplus proceeds invested in U.S. debt securities. By 1975, all OPEC members followed suit. This arrangement has sustained dollar dominance for five decades—as of Q1 2026, the dollar still accounts for 57.13% of global foreign exchange reserves (the latest confirmed IMF COFER data; earlier provisional figures for Q2 2026 showed 56.32% but have been revised).

However, the system is under strain. Central banks are inching away from the dollar, with 74% expecting dollar allocations to fall over the next five years. Gold holdings have risen, with 82% of central banks now holding physical gold. The renminbi is expected to account for 5% of average portfolios in 10 years. Geopolitical fragmentation, U.S. fiscal deficits, and the weaponization of dollar infrastructure have accelerated de-dollarization discussions. The Bretton Woods institutions themselves face a "rupture in world order" that challenges their legitimacy.

## Part 1: The Creation (1944)

### 1.1 The Gathering

In July 1944—nine months before the end of World War II in Europe—representatives of 44 nations assembled at the Mount Washington Hotel in Bretton Woods, New Hampshire. The United Nations Monetary and Financial Conference was an unprecedented cooperative effort for nations that had been erecting trade barriers for more than a decade.

### 1.2 The Architects

| Figure | Role | Proposal |
|--------|------|----------|
| **John Maynard Keynes** | British Treasury Adviser | "Clearing Union" with a new global currency called the "bancor"; a global central bank with authority to intervene in crises; surplus countries penalized for hoarding; symmetric adjustment mechanism |
| **Harry Dexter White** | U.S. Treasury Chief International Economist | "Stabilization Fund" with finite resources; the U.S. dollar as the anchor; more limited institutional powers |

The U.S. vision prevailed. Keynes's bancor was rejected; White's dollar-centric system was adopted. The bancor concept has periodically resurfaced in reform discussions, most notably in proposals to expand the IMF's Special Drawing Rights (SDRs), which are a partial, underutilized echo of Keynes's original vision.

### 1.3 The Institutions Created

| Institution | Purpose |
|-------------|---------|
| **International Monetary Fund (IMF)** | Monitor exchange rates; provide temporary loans to countries with balance of payments deficits; preside over a multilateral payments system |
| **International Bank for Reconstruction and Development (IBRD / World Bank)** | Long-term credits for postwar reconstruction; subsequent development assistance |

The rules for international trade—the "third leg" of the triad—were not on the agenda at Bretton Woods. The General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (GATT) emerged later and eventually became the World Trade Organization in 1995.

### 1.4 How the System Worked

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    THE BRETTON WOODS SYSTEM                 │
│                                                             │
│   All currencies → pegged to → U.S. dollar                  │
│                                                             │
│   U.S. dollar → pegged to → gold at $35/oz                  │
│                                                             │
│   Only foreign governments and central banks                │
│   could redeem dollars for gold                             │
│                                                             │
│   IMF would lend reserves (usually dollars) to              │
│   enable central banks to maintain parity                   │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

Countries fixed their exchange rates relative to the U.S. dollar. The United States promised to provide gold on demand in exchange for dollars at $35 per ounce. Member governments undertook to maintain a fixed parity for their exchange rate unless facing "fundamental disequilibrium". The IMF would lend reserves to help central banks maintain parity without resorting to "measures destructive of national or international prosperity".

### 1.5 The Initial Success

The system worked reasonably well for over two decades. The world economy grew rapidly, recessions were generally minor, and Keynesian economic policies enabled governments to dampen economic fluctuations. However, strains started to show in the 1960s.

### 1.6 The Geopolitical Frame

Bretton Woods was the financial counterpart to U.S. hegemony—Pax Americana—replacing the British sterling system that had dominated the 19th and early 20th centuries. French President Valéry Giscard d'Estaing later coined the phrase "exorbitant privilege" to describe the advantage the dollar's reserve status gave the United States, allowing it to fund deficits, military spending, and alliances with cheaply printed currency.

## Part 2: The Fatal Flaw (1960)

### 2.1 The Dollar Glut

By 1960, the postwar "dollar gap"—a shortage of dollars abroad—had become a "dollar glut". Continuous U.S. balance of payments deficits during the 1950s had provided the world with liquidity but had also caused dollar reserves to build up in the central banks of Europe and Japan. As these central banks redeemed dollars for gold, U.S. gold reserves dipped dangerously low.

### 2.2 Triffin's Dilemma

Testifying before the U.S. Congress in 1960, Belgian economist Robert Triffin exposed the system's fundamental contradiction:

| The Problem | The Contradiction |
|-------------|-------------------|
| If U.S. deficits stopped | The international community would lose its largest source of additions to reserves; liquidity shortage would pull the world economy into a contractionary spiral |
| If U.S. deficits continued | A steady stream of dollars would fuel growth, but excessive deficits would erode confidence in the dollar's value; without confidence, the dollar would no longer be accepted as the world's reserve currency; the system would break down |

The core of the dilemma is that the issuer of a reserve currency must serve two different sets of users: the domestic economy and the international economy. The conflict of economic interests between short-term domestic and long-term international objectives is inherent in the system.

### 2.3 Triffin's Solution

Triffin proposed the creation of new reserve units that would not depend on gold or currencies but would add to the world's total liquidity. This would allow the United States to reduce its balance of payments deficits while still allowing for global economic expansion. The proposal was not adopted, though the IMF's Special Drawing Rights (SDRs), created in 1969, were a partial implementation of this idea.

### 2.4 The System's Instability

Experience during the 1960s showed that the artificial stability of fixed exchange rates could delay necessary adjustment, ultimately ending in traumatic balance-of-payments crises. The rise of private capital flows meant that any hint of a possible devaluation would send billions of dollars out of a deficit country.

## Part 3: The Collapse (1971)

### 3.1 The Pressures

By the early 1970s, multiple pressures converged:

| Pressure | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **U.S. inflation** | Rising U.S. inflation since 1965 created growing international imbalances |
| **Vietnam War spending** | The costs of the Vietnam War strained U.S. finances |
| **"Great Society" programs** | Domestic spending added to fiscal pressures |
| **Gold outflows** | The United States did not have enough gold to cover the volume of dollars in worldwide circulation at $35 per ounce |
| **Surplus country reluctance** | Germany and Japan refused to revalue their currencies |

As Treasury Secretary John Connally put it: "Providing reserves and exchanges for the whole world is too much for one country and one currency to bear".

### 3.2 The Nixon Shock

On August 15, 1971, President Richard Nixon announced his New Economic Policy. The key measures:

| Measure | Purpose |
|---------|---------|
| **Suspension of dollar convertibility into gold** | Close the "gold window"; end the dollar's gold backing |
| **90-day freeze on prices and wages** | Stop the rise in the cost of living |
| **10% tariff on all dutiable imports** | Induce trading partners to adjust their currencies upward |

Nixon framed it as a program "to create a new prosperity without war". Though intended as a ploy to force surplus countries to revalue their currencies, since the dollar was the lynchpin of the system, closing the gold window effectively ended the Bretton Woods system.

### 3.3 The Smithsonian Agreement (1971)

A short-lived attempt to salvage the system occurred in December 1971 at the Smithsonian Institution in Washington, D.C. The Group of Ten (G-10) nations agreed to a new set of fixed parities, with the dollar devalued to $38 per ounce of gold and other currencies revalued. The agreement was intended as a temporary fix but collapsed within 18 months, as speculative attacks on currencies continued.

### 3.4 The Float (1973)

By February 1973, the second dollar devaluation was announced. By March 1973, the major currencies were floating against each other, ushering in the era of floating exchange rates that continues to this day. The fixed exchange rate system had ended.

### 3.5 The Aftermath

The Nixon Shock was a critical event in the history of the international monetary system, on par with the ending of the classical gold standard at the outbreak of World War I. While a success at home, Nixon's speech shocked many abroad, who saw it as an act of worrisome unilateralism. The collapse prompted a fundamental rethink about what would give stability to the international monetary system.

## Part 4: The Petrodollar System (1974)

### 4.1 The Crisis

With the dollar no longer backed by gold, confidence in the currency began to crack. Saudi Arabia, receiving dollars for oil shipments, could no longer convert them to gold at a guaranteed price. The Saudis began secretly dumping dollars and buying gold on the London market. At the same time, the OPEC oil embargo and dramatic production cuts quadrupled the price of oil, plunging the U.S. into a national energy crisis.

### 4.2 The Kissinger-Saudi Agreement (1974)

In 1974, President Nixon and Secretary of State Henry Kissinger entered negotiations with the Saudi Royal Family. The agreement:

| U.S. Obligations | Saudi Obligations |
|------------------|-------------------|
| Provide arms and military assistance | Price all oil sales in U.S. dollars |
| Assist with protection of oil fields | Invest surplus oil proceeds in U.S. debt securities |

Behind this agreement was significant realpolitik pressure. According to top-secret documents declassified by the British government in 2014, the U.S. communicated a not-so-subtle threat to invade Saudi Arabia and take the oil by force if the deal was not concluded. While historians debate the precise weight of this threat versus mutual interests, the agreement was clearly a foundational moment in the U.S.-Saudi relationship.

**Critical nuance**: Oil was already largely dollar-denominated before 1974. The agreement formalized and deepened this practice, expanding petrodollar recycling and solidifying the "exorbitant privilege" of the dollar.

### 4.3 The System Expands

By 1975, all oil-producing members of OPEC followed suit. Consequently, all other important commodities began to trade in dollars. Petrodollars have since continuously flown back into the U.S. economy, creating investment and economic growth that might not otherwise occur.

The system has been described by critics as "a classic mafia style arrangement"—in exchange for Saudi Arabia's agreement to the sole use of the dollar for oil transactions, the U.S. underwrote Saudi Arabia's security. Defenders note that the deal provided stability to global oil markets and aligned U.S. and Saudi strategic interests for decades.

## Part 5: Dollar Dominance in 2026

### 5.1 Current Status

Despite decades of de-dollarization talk, the dollar remains dominant:

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| **Share of global reserves (Q1 2026)** | 57.13% | IMF COFER data (latest confirmed) |
| **Share of global reserves (historical peak)** | ~71% (approximate peak) | IMF historical data |
| **Share of international transactions** | ~88-90% | SWIFT / BIS estimates |
| **Reserve share relative to U.S. economy** | More than double the U.S.'s 26% share of global economy | OMFIF |

The dollar still has more than double the share of reserves relative to the U.S.'s share of the global economy. Dollar defenders point to the unparalleled depth and liquidity of its safe asset market, broader dollar-denominated securities markets, and the infrastructure to exchange, hedge, store and remit dollars.

### 5.2 The Shift Underway

Central banks are inching away from the dollar:

| Indicator | Data |
|-----------|------|
| **Net intention to divest dollars (1-2 years)** | 3% | OMFIF 2026 Global Public Investor survey |
| **Net intention to divest dollars (10-year horizon)** | 8% | OMFIF survey |
| **Central banks expecting dollar allocations to fall (5 years)** | 74% | World Gold Council survey |
| **Proportion of central banks now holding physical gold** | 82% (up from 71% last year) | World Gold Council survey |

Asked to pan out a 10-year horizon, respondents show a net decrease of 8%. Reserve managers are spreading different dollar functions across a set of partial alternatives: the dollar remains the liquidity anchor, gold is the strategic hedge, the euro has the strongest potential if Europe can address the safe-asset gap, and the renminbi is the trade-linked emerging-market alternative.

### 5.3 The Drivers of De-Dollarization

| Concern | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **U.S. fiscal deficits** | Persistent deficits erode confidence in the dollar |
| **Currency depreciation** | Risk of inflation and devaluation |
| **Weaponization of dollar infrastructure** | Freezing of Russian central bank reserves after Ukraine invasion (2022) sharpened concerns for non-U.S. allies |
| **Geopolitical fragmentation** | Rising multipolarity drives diversification |
| **Sanctions risk** | Countries fear being cut off from dollar system |

### 5.4 Gold as the Beneficiary

Gold has emerged as the primary alternative:

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| **Central banks citing geopolitical risk as reason for holding gold** | 51% (up from 45% in 2025) |
| **Central banks citing diversification as reason for holding gold** | 68% (up from 61%) |
| **Central banks now holding physical gold** | 82% (up from 71% last year) |

Returns are only a marginal motivation—10% cite higher relative returns as a reason for holding gold. Geopolitical risk and diversification are the primary drivers.

### 5.5 The Renminbi's Rise

The renminbi is expected to account for **5%** of the average portfolio in 10 years, with a net 13% of central banks intending to increase holdings over the next 12-24 months. China's yuan-priced oil benchmark backed by gold represents a decisive step in creating a viable alternative to what some call the "tyranny of the dollar".

### 5.6 Historical Reserve Share Trends

| Year | Dollar Share of Global Reserves |
|------|--------------------------------|
| ~2000 | ~71% (approximate peak) |
| 2010 | ~62% |
| 2020 | ~59% |
| 2025 | ~58% |
| Q1 2026 | 57.13% |

The gradual decline has been steady but slow. The dollar is not collapsing, but its dominance is eroding incrementally.

## Part 6: The Bretton Woods Institutions Under Pressure

### 6.1 IMF and World Bank Today

Both the IMF and World Bank are still operating today. However, the "post-Bretton Woods system" remains centered on the U.S. dollar, suffering from the Triffin dilemma and what some call the "new Triffin dilemma," with the dollar's excessive privileges causing global financial turmoil.

### 6.2 Calls for Reform

There are growing calls for reform:

| Proposal | Source |
|----------|--------|
| A new "Bretton Woods" conference should redraft the institutional basis of the IMF and World Bank | Chatham House |
| The postwar Bretton Woods system has struggled to adequately manage internationally harmful spillovers | Bretton Woods Committee |
| Keynes's bancor idea needs revisiting | ABC News |

### 6.3 The Legitimacy Crisis

The IMF and World Bank Spring Meetings in 2026 were framed by a "rupture in world order" that further challenges the institutions' legitimacy. As one analysis notes, "the extractive foundations of Bretton Woods" included hidden dimensions of racial politics in monetary order, such as the role of South African gold mining.

**Critical nuance**: While the "racial politics/extractive foundations" argument is made by some critical scholars (e.g., on the role of South African gold in U.S. dollar backing), the mainstream critique of the IMF/World Bank centers on governance (voting shares favoring the West and U.S. effective veto power), conditionality (austerity policies), and relevance to a multipolar world. Both critiques are valid but serve different analytical purposes.

### 6.4 The U.S. Veto

The United States holds a **de facto veto** over IMF decisions, as it controls just over 16% of voting shares—the threshold for major decisions requiring an 85% supermajority. This governance structure is increasingly resented by emerging economies.

## Part 7: Broader Implications — Geopolitics, Domestic Politics, and Scenarios

### 7.1 Geopolitical Transition

Bretton Woods was the financial architecture of Pax Americana. Its erosion is a measure of U.S. relative decline and the rise of a multipolar world:

| Actor | Role in Post-Bretton Woods System |
|-------|-----------------------------------|
| **United States** | Traditional hegemon; dollar issuer; IMF/World Bank veto holder |
| **China** | Rising challenger; yuan internationalization; BRICS+ alternative institutions |
| **Russia** | Sanctioned aggressor; accelerating de-dollarization; gold accumulation |
| **India** | Swing state; hedging between U.S. and China |
| **BRICS+** | Institutional alternative to IMF/World Bank (New Development Bank, Contingent Reserve Arrangement) |

### 7.2 Domestic U.S. Political Economy

The Triffin Dilemma persists in a different form. The U.S. runs chronic deficits to supply global liquidity. This fuels:

- **Asset bubbles** — as surplus dollars seek returns
- **Inequality debates** — as financial assets concentrate wealth
- **Political backlash** — "America First" critiques of global entanglements
- **Military-industrial-financial complex** — petrodollar recycling finances defense spending

**Counter-narrative**: Many U.S. policymakers view dollar dominance as a net strategic asset—it enables sanctions power, reduces borrowing costs, and provides geopolitical leverage. The dollar's "exorbitant privilege" has significant benefits for the U.S. economy.

### 7.3 Scenarios for the Future

| Scenario | Likelihood | Description |
|----------|------------|-------------|
| **Fragmentation** | Moderate | De-dollarization accelerates, led by BRICS+; dollar share falls below 50% in 10-15 years; alternative payment systems (mBridge, CBDCs) gain traction |
| **Resilience** | High | Dollar maintains dominance (55-60%) due to depth of U.S. markets, lack of credible alternatives; gradual diversification continues |
| **Abrupt Shock** | Low but rising | Sudden loss of confidence (e.g., U.S. default, geopolitical crisis); dollar crashes; global liquidity crisis; IMF forced to intervene |
| **Muddle Through** | Most Likely | Slow erosion; dollar remains dominant but loses share; multipolar reserve system emerges (dollar, euro, yuan, gold, SDRs) |

### 7.4 The Petrodollar Endgame

The petrodollar system faces multiple pressures:

| Pressure | Impact |
|----------|--------|
| **Green transition** | Oil's relative importance in global trade declines; reduces demand for dollar pricing |
| **China's yuan oil benchmark** | Offers alternative to dollar pricing for oil trades |
| **Geopolitical fragmentation** | BRICS+ countries increasingly trade in non-dollar currencies |
| **U.S. fiscal trajectory** | Growing deficits and debt erode confidence in dollar assets |

**The question is not whether the petrodollar will end, but whether it will be replaced by a multipolar system or something else.**
