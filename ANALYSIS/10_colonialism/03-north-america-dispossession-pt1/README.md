## Executive Summary

The colonization of North America began with competing European claims (Spain, France, Britain, Netherlands) but coalesced into a British/American project of systematic Indigenous dispossession. The **Doctrine of Discovery** (1493 papal bulls) provided religious and legal justification for seizing lands inhabited by non‑Christians. From the **Royal Proclamation of 1763** (which *theoretically* protected Native lands) to the **Treaty of Paris (1783)** and subsequent U.S. treaty system, Indigenous nations were gradually pushed west, then onto reservations, then erased through assimilation policies.

**Key Finding:** The legal frameworks for Indigenous dispossession in North America were established before the U.S. even existed, and were refined continuously — treaties were used as instruments of land transfer, not genuine nation‑to‑nation agreements.

## Part 1: Timeline — Early Phase (1492–1800s)

| Year | Event | Key Actors | Impact |
|------|-------|------------|--------|
| **1493** | Papal Bull *Inter Caetera* (Doctrine of Discovery) | Pope Alexander VI | Gave Catholic monarchs (Spain/Portugal) rights to colonize “non‑Christian” lands; later used by Britain/U.S. |
| **1497** | John Cabot claims Newfoundland for England | England | First English claim in North America |
| **1534** | Jacques Cartier claims St. Lawrence Valley for France | France | Foundation of New France (Quebec) |
| **1607** | Jamestown colony established | England | First permanent English settlement; Powhatan Confederacy begins resisting |
| **1620** | Plymouth (Mayflower) — Pilgrims | England | Wampanoag initially assist; later war and dispossession |
| **1763** | Royal Proclamation | British Crown | Set aside lands west of Appalachians for Indigenous peoples — **the first “reservation” concept**; immediately violated by settlers |
| **1778** | First U.S. treaty with Indigenous nation (Delaware) | U.S. Continental Congress | Established pattern of “recognizing” tribes in exchange for land cessions |
| **1783** | Treaty of Paris (ends U.S. Revolution) | U.S., Britain | Britain ceded Indigenous lands (without Indigenous consent) to U.S.; legal precedent for “conquest” |
| **1790** | Indian Intercourse Act | U.S. Congress | Regulated trade and land purchases — *required* federal approval for all land deals; widely ignored |
| **1803** | Louisiana Purchase | U.S., France | Doubled U.S. territory; all Indigenous claims in that territory were considered “void” by the U.S. |
| **1823** | *Johnson v. McIntosh* (Supreme Court) | U.S. Supreme Court (John Marshall) | Ruled that Indigenous peoples had *“right of occupancy”* but not *“right of title”* — the U.S. held ultimate title via discovery doctrine. This is the **foundational theft ruling**. |
| **1830** | Indian Removal Act (end of early period) | U.S. Congress, Andrew Jackson | Legalized forced removal of Southeastern tribes (Cherokee, Choctaw, etc.) — leads to Trail of Tears |

## Part 3: Key Legal Mechanisms of Dispossession

| Mechanism | Description | Modern Legacy |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| **Doctrine of Discovery** | Papal bulls + subsequent court rulings | Still cited in U.S. law; never repudiated by Supreme Court |
| **Treaties** | Bilateral “nation‑to‑nation” agreements | Most broken; tribes still fight for treaty rights in courts |
| **Allotment (later, Dawes Act 1887)** | Breaking communal land into individual parcels; surplus sold to whites | Fractionated land ownership; loss of 90 million acres |
| **"Right of Occupancy" vs. "Title"** | Legal distinction invented by Marshall | Tribes are “domestic dependent nations” — not fully sovereign |
| **Manifest Destiny** | Ideological justification for expansion | Embedded in U.S. exceptionalism; still affects policy |
