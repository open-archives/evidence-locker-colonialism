## Executive Summary

The People's Republic of China has a unique position in the colonial narrative: it was itself a victim of Western colonialism, carved into spheres of influence by the Opium Wars (1839–42, 1856–60) and subjected to a century of "unequal treaties" that stripped it of sovereignty, territory, and economic control. This history of subjugation has bequeathed to China a "strong legacy of anti-colonialism," which Beijing invokes to distinguish the Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) from the exploitative colonial projects of the British and French empires.

Yet the question at the heart of this entry is whether the BRI represents a genuine departure from colonial patterns — or a repetition of them, with Chinese characteristics. The BRI, launched in 2013, is a multi-trillion-dollar infrastructure and economic development initiative spanning Asia, Africa, Latin America, and Europe. Proponents frame it as "win-win" South-South cooperation. Critics argue that it operates "in the mould of erstwhile European colonial powers of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, especially Great Britain," securing resources, trade routes, export markets, and overseas bases.

**Key Finding**: The China case presents a profound irony — a former colonized nation projecting economic power across the Global South through mechanisms that, in key respects, mirror the infrastructure-extraction-debt cycles of 19th-century colonialism. Whether this constitutes "neocolonialism" or a novel form of development cooperation depends largely on whether one emphasizes outcomes (debt dependency, resource extraction, loss of sovereignty) or intentions (anti-colonial self-narrative, infrastructure provision, non-interference).

**Added Irony (2026 Update)**: The British Empire once waged war to force opium into China. Today, China is the dominant global source of fentanyl precursors fueling North America's opioid crisis, while Chinese influence operations (including alleged overseas police stations) have been documented in countries such as Canada. The historical wheel has turned.

## Part 1: Historical Parallel — Opium Wars to BRI

### 1.1 The Opium Wars: Colonial Template

The Opium Wars were two armed conflicts in China in the mid-19th century between Western powers and the Qing dynasty. The **First Opium War (1839–42)** was fought between China and Britain; the **Second Opium War (1856–60)**, also known as the Arrow War, was fought by Britain and France against China.

**Origins**: Foreign traders (primarily British) had been illegally exporting opium from India to China since the 18th century, but the trade grew dramatically from about 1820. The resulting widespread addiction in China caused serious social and economic disruption. In spring 1839, Chinese Imperial Commissioner Lin Zexu confiscated and destroyed more than 20,000 chests of opium — some 1,400 tons — warehoused at Canton by British merchants.

**The British Response**: Rather than accept China's right to regulate a destructive drug trade, the British government — which did not wish its subjects to be tried in the Chinese legal system — refused to turn accused British sailors over to Chinese courts after they killed a Chinese villager. Hostilities broke out when British warships destroyed a Chinese blockade of the Pearl River estuary. The British fleet captured Nanjing in August 1842, ending the war.

**The Treaty of Nanjing (1842)**: China paid Britain an indemnity of $21 million, ceded the territory of Hong Kong, agreed to establish a "fair and reasonable" tariff, and opened five "treaty ports" where British merchants could trade and reside. The treaty marked the start of the era of "unequal treaties" that weakened Qing sovereignty and helped ultimately topple the dynasty.

**The Second Opium War (1856–60)**: Triggered by the Arrow incident — when Canton police boarded a British-registered ship and charged its crew with smuggling — the war saw British and French forces occupy Canton, reach Tianjin, and eventually capture Beijing, where they burned the emperor's summer palace.

### 1.2 The Colonial Pattern: Infrastructure, Extraction, Debt, and Control

The 19th-century colonial project in China followed a consistent template:

| Element | Colonial Form | Modern BRI Parallel |
|---------|---------------|---------------------|
| **Market Access** | Treaty ports forced open by gunboat diplomacy | Infrastructure projects (ports, railways) that facilitate Chinese market access |
| **Debt** | Indemnities imposed on defeated China ($21M in 1842) | Sovereign loans to BRI countries for infrastructure projects |
| **Resource Extraction** | Opium, tea, silk, porcelain | Minerals, energy, agricultural commodities |
| **Territorial Control** | Hong Kong ceded (1842); spheres of influence | Ports (Gwadar, Hambantota), special economic zones |
| **Legal Privilege** | Extraterritoriality — British subjects tried in British courts | Chinese contractors operating under Chinese law; arbitration clauses in BRI contracts |
| **Infrastructure** | Railways, telegraph lines built by foreign powers | Railways, ports, digital infrastructure built by Chinese state-owned enterprises |
| **Ideological Justification** | "Civilizing mission," free trade | "Win-win cooperation," South-South solidarity |

### 1.3 The Irony of Anti-Colonialism

China's history of being exploited by colonial powers from the 19th and early 20th centuries has bequeathed a strong legacy of anti-colonialism. This is why, Chinese officials and scholars argue, over 150 countries have signed BRI cooperation documents — they trust the BRI because it is "unlike the exploitative colonial activities" of the British and American empires.

Yet scholars have noted that the BRI "rebrands China's colonization of parts of Central Asia as peaceful and mutually beneficial. Yet behind this narrative lies a legacy of conquest, repression, and demographic engineering, particularly in Xinjiang."

### 1.4 The Continuity Question

The central question of this entry is whether the BRI represents:

1. **A clean break** — a post-colonial, South-South cooperation model that genuinely differs from 19th-century colonialism;
2. **A repetition** — the same infrastructure-extraction-debt-sovereignty patterns, now with China as the metropolitan power; or
3. **Something in between** — a hybrid that combines developmental benefits with neocolonial dynamics.

Analysts have drawn explicit parallels. One thesis "aims to determine the strategic motivations behind the Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) by performing process tracing with historical comparative analysis of the BRI against the Dutch Vereenigde Oostindische Compagnie (VOC), the British East India Company (EIC), and America's Marshall Plan." The China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC) has been described by observers as potentially becoming the "New East India Company," effectively turning Pakistan into a Chinese client state.

### 1.5 The Fentanyl Turn: Full Circle

The ultimate irony: the British Empire fought the Opium Wars to force opium into China. By 2026, China has become the world's largest producer of fentanyl precursors — and the U.S. has accused China of fueling the American opioid crisis. The colonial power that once weaponized narcotics against China now finds itself on the receiving end of a similar dynamic, with China as the supplier.

## Part 2: The Fentanyl Turn — "Turning the Tables" or Profit-Driven Flow?

### 2.1 Narrative Extension of the Opium Wars
Chinese state media and officials frequently cite the Opium Wars as the beginning of national humiliation and a cautionary tale of Western drug warfare. Some Western analysts and policymakers now invert the narrative: China, once the victim of forced narcotics, has become a central node in the global supply of synthetic opioids.

### 2.2 Factual Landscape (2025–2026)
China's massive chemical and pharmaceutical sector makes it a primary global source of fentanyl precursors and related equipment.
- Following 2019 class-wide controls on fentanyl substances, direct shipments of finished fentanyl from China to the United States dropped sharply. Production largely shifted to Mexico, relying on Chinese-origin precursors.
- US intelligence continues to identify China (along with India) as the main source countries for precursors used by transnational criminal organizations.

**China's Official Position**: The opioid crisis is primarily a problem of Western demand. China has strengthened domestic scheduling, cooperated with international partners (including post-2025 US summits), and imposed export controls on specific chemicals to North America.

**Critical Perspectives**: Despite regulatory efforts, precursor flows continue. Some view this as hybrid warfare, economic retaliation, or at minimum negligent tolerance of criminal networks within China's vast industrial base.

## Part 4: Key Colonial/Neocolonial Mechanisms — Historical and Modern

| Mechanism | Historical Form (Opium Wars Era) | Modern Equivalent (BRI) |
|-----------|----------------------------------|-------------------------|
| **Debt as control** | Indemnities imposed on defeated China ($21M, 1842) | Sovereign loans to BRI countries; "debt-trap diplomacy" debate |
| **Infrastructure as penetration** | Railways and telegraphs built by foreign powers in treaty ports | Ports (Gwadar, Hambantota), railways (Laos-China), highways, digital infrastructure |
| **Resource extraction** | Opium, tea, silk, porcelain | Minerals (copper in Zambia, lithium in Latin America), energy, agricultural land |
| **Territorial footholds** | Hong Kong (ceded 1842); spheres of influence | Port leases (Gwadar, Hambantota); Special Economic Zones; overseas bases (Djibouti) |
| **Legal asymmetry** | Extraterritoriality — foreign nationals tried in their own courts | Chinese contractors under Chinese law; dispute resolution in Chinese courts; arbitration clauses |
| **Ideological justification** | "Civilizing mission," free trade | "Win-win cooperation," South-South solidarity, anti-colonialism |
| **Economic dependency** | China became dependent on foreign trade on Western terms | BRI countries become dependent on Chinese loans, construction, and markets |
