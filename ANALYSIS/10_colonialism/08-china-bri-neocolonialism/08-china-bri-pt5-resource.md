## 5.1 The Colonial Extraction Template

The 19th-century colonial project was, at its core, an extraction machine. Rubber from the Congo, palm oil from West Africa, tin from Malaya, copper from Chile, guano from Peru, jade and rubies from Burma—all flowed to metropolitan powers through infrastructure built expressly to move resources from colony to factory. The extraction was often brutal: the Belgian Congo's rubber quotas were enforced by mutilation; the British Empire's opium monopoly in India funded colonial administration while addicting China.

**The colonial pattern** was consistent: infrastructure (railways, ports) → extraction (minerals, agriculture, timber) → export (to metropolitan markets) → enrichment (of colonial powers and their corporations). Local populations provided labor, bore environmental costs, and received minimal benefit.

**The BRI extraction model** mirrors this structure in key respects—but with Chinese characteristics. Chinese state-owned enterprises (SOEs) build ports and railways (infrastructure), secure mining concessions (extraction), ship raw or semi-processed materials to China (export), and generate returns for Chinese banks and corporations (enrichment). The rhetoric is "win-win" and "South-South cooperation"; the structural reality, in many cases, is a resource extraction chain that benefits China disproportionately.

**Key distinction**: Unlike 19th-century colonialism, China does not formally colonize or impose direct political administration. The extraction occurs through contracts, loans, and equity stakes—a "neocolonial" rather than colonial model. Whether this represents a meaningful improvement depends on whether one emphasizes process (consent, contracts) or outcomes (dependency, environmental degradation, limited local value retention).

## 5.2 Africa — Copper, Cobalt, Oil, and Lithium

Africa is the frontline of China's resource extraction strategy. China has been Africa's largest trading partner since 2009, and the resource intensity of that trade has only deepened. In 2024, Chinese investment in African metals and mining hit a record $21.4 billion, up 10% from 2023. By early 2026, a small number of African countries—DRC (cobalt), Angola (oil), Guinea (bauxite), and South Africa (critical minerals)—captured the bulk of export gains to China.

### 5.2.1 Democratic Republic of Congo — Cobalt and Copper

The DRC is the world's largest producer of cobalt (over 70% of global supply) and a major copper producer. Chinese firms now play a dominant role in the country's extractive industry, controlling major assets such as Tenke Fungurume, one of the world's largest copper-cobalt mines, majority-owned by China Molybdenum. Chinese firms control roughly 70–80% of industrial cobalt production in the DRC, with the remainder held by Western and state-owned enterprises.

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Key minerals** | Cobalt (70%+ global supply), copper |
| **Chinese players** | China Molybdenum (Tenke Fungurume), Zijin Mining, Huayou Cobalt |
| **Model** | Resource-backed loans; integrated mine-to-refinery investments |
| **Outcome (2026)** | Chinese firms control dominant share of DRC's cobalt and copper production; mining sector is backbone of DRC-China trade |
| **Colonial parallel** | Belgian Congo's rubber and copper extraction (Force Publique, concession companies) vs. Chinese SOE-dominated mining |

The DRC case illustrates the **integrated resource-access model**: Chinese entities combine financing, infrastructure development, and diplomatic relations to create a robust framework for securing resources. This goes beyond simple extraction—it is a vertically integrated strategy from mine to port to refinery.

### 5.2.2 Angola — The Oil-Backed Loan Architecture

Angola is the most extensively documented case of China's oil-backed loan model, where Chinese financing was collateralized against future oil production.

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Key resource** | Oil |
| **Model** | Oil-backed loans (collateralized against future production) |
| **Debt status** | Angola is Africa's largest debtor to China |
| **Risk** | Collateralization terms create commodity revenue risks; during price downturns, consequences are severe |
| **Outcome (2026)** | Angola remains heavily indebted; China has replicated the Angola model in other resource-rich African countries |

The Angola model has been replicated across Africa: infrastructure loans are collateralized against future resource exports, creating a cycle where debt servicing depends on continued extraction. When commodity prices fall, the debt burden becomes unsustainable—but the collateral (mines, oil fields, or future production) remains under Chinese control.

### 5.2.3 Zambia — Copper and the Resource Curse

Zambia's copper-for-infrastructure deals were detailed in Part 3. The key update as of 2026: Zambia's debt-to-GDP stands at approximately 120%, with Chinese loans a significant component. The restructuring negotiations (2023–2024) resulted in Chinese firms taking control of struggling copper mines—a classic resource-for-debt swap.

### 5.2.4 Guinea — Bauxite and Iron Ore

Guinea holds the world's largest bauxite reserves and major iron ore deposits. The $23 billion Simandou iron ore project, anchored by Chinese equity, delivered its first shipment to China in January 2026. Once fully ramped up by 2029, Simandou will significantly alter global iron ore supply dynamics—and China will control a major share.

### 5.2.5 Zimbabwe — Lithium

Zimbabwe has emerged as a strategic lithium anchor for China, with Chinese investments securing mining concessions and processing capacity. As of 2026, Zimbabwe is moving to ban raw mineral exports to force domestic processing—a policy shift that aligns with China's preference for local refining (which Chinese firms often control).

### 5.2.6 The Processing Bottleneck — China's Real Power

The most consequential fact about China's resource strategy is not extraction—it is **processing**. China processes:
- Over 90% of rare earth elements
- Almost 100% of graphite
- Over 75% of cobalt
- Over 60% of lithium

This is the real strategic power: China doesn't just extract; it controls the chokepoint of refinement, meaning it can dictate global prices and supply regardless of who owns the mines. This means that even if African or Latin American countries retain ownership of their mines, the *value addition*—refining, processing, and manufacturing—occurs in China. This is the 21st-century equivalent of colonial-era processing: raw materials leave the Global South, and finished goods return at higher prices.

## 5.3 Latin America — Lithium, Copper, and the New Extraction

Latin America's resource wealth has long been extracted by foreign powers—Spanish silver, British guano and nitrates, U.S. copper and oil. China is the latest in this lineage, but with a distinctive feature: it is acquiring not just resources but the *entire value chain*.

### 5.3.1 The Lithium Triangle — Argentina, Bolivia, Chile

The "Lithium Triangle"—Argentina, Bolivia, and Chile—holds over 50% of the world's lithium reserves, critical for electric vehicle batteries and energy storage. Chinese capital has moved aggressively into the region.

| Country | Chinese Presence | Status (2026) |
|---------|------------------|---------------|
| **Argentina** | Multiple Chinese firms; dozens of new projects planned | Argentina has planned dozens of new lithium projects; projected capacity may exceed 700,000 tons of lithium carbonate equivalent by 2030 |
| **Chile** | Major Chinese imports; state-owned Codelco's salt flat strategy | In November 2025, China imported 10,800 tons of lithium carbonate from Chile—49% of total Chinese lithium imports; Chilean salt flat nationalization has slowed progress |
| **Bolivia** | Chinese interest; shifting toward U.S. partnership | Under new U.S. policy, Bolivia has shifted toward U.S. partnership |

Chinese firms participate across the lithium value chain in Argentina, Chile, Colombia, and Peru. The framework of analysis is **neo-extractivism**: the rhetoric of green development and South-South cooperation coexists with the formation of extraction-dependent economies.

### 5.3.2 Peru — Copper

Peru is a major copper producer, and Chinese firms have secured significant holdings. The Las Bambas copper mine, acquired by Chinese SOE MMG, was financed by a $2.5 billion loan from Top Create Resources, a Chinese SOE. Chinese official sector creditors act as "relationship bankers," offering a series of consecutive loans for the development and expansion of mines.

### 5.3.3 The Colonial Echo in Latin America

The parallel to 19th-century extraction is explicit in the academic literature. One study analyzes Chinese capital in the Lithium Triangle through the framework of dependency theory, arguing that lithium extraction occurs within a context of neo-extractivism. Another examines China's role as a "learning raw materials empire" that is increasingly controlling global value and supply chains.

The question is whether Latin American countries can avoid the resource curse—the paradox where resource wealth leads to economic stagnation, inequality, and conflict. China's presence in the extractive sector creates new dynamics that need to be reevaluated in light of its direct and indirect impacts on resource-endowed countries.

## 5.4 Asia — Nickel, Rare Earths, and Strategic Minerals

### 5.4.1 Indonesia — Nickel and the BRI Showcase

Indonesia's nickel boom is one of the BRI's signature resource stories. China-led foreign direct investment has been the main catalyst behind Indonesia's nickel boom, enabling a rapid build-out of smelters and industrial parks.

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Total Chinese investment** | Over $14 billion |
| **Key project** | IMIP (Indonesia Morowali Industrial Park) — total investment reached ~$41.48 billion by end of 2025 |
| **Model** | Chinese firms built complete industrial system: from geological exploration to mine development to hydrometallurgical refining to battery material processing |
| **Outcome (2026)** | China has become Indonesia's indispensable nickel partner |

**Tension (2026)**: In 2026, Indonesia's new government slashed nickel mining quotas from 379 million tons to approximately 250–270 million tons—a cut of over 30%. This was perceived as an attempt to "harvest" Chinese investments. Chinese firms responded by dismantling production lines and returning to China. The episode illustrates that resource extraction relationships are not one-way—host countries can push back, but at the cost of investment and technology.

### 5.4.2 Myanmar — Rare Earths, Jade, and Conflict Minerals

Myanmar is one of the world's most significant sources of mined rare earth elements. China is the largest buyer of Myanmar's jade, though raw stones are overwhelmingly smuggled to Yunnan rather than going through legal channels.

| Resource | Chinese Role | Conflict Dimension |
|----------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Rare earths** | Myanmar's rare earth output flows overwhelmingly into Chinese processing facilities | The Kachin Independence Army seized rare earth mines from junta-aligned militias near the Chinese border in October 2024 and has reportedly resumed exports to China |
| **Jade** | China is the largest buyer; smuggling is rampant | Conflict in Kachin State fuels the illicit jade trade |
| **Strategic value** | Rare earths and tin ore are essential to high-technology and defense manufacturing | China's interests are "not on federalism, but rare earths, infrastructure, mining, and securing the economic corridor to the Indian Ocean" |

Myanmar illustrates the **conflict resource** dimension of BRI extraction: China's resource interests entangle it with Myanmar's junta, armed groups, and civil war. China's strategic intervention in post-coup Myanmar presents "a complex mix of geopolitical ambition, economic necessity, and security entanglement".

### 5.4.3 Central Asia — Oil, Gas, and Rare Earths

Central Asian republics (Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, Turkmenistan) are major oil and gas producers, with Chinese pipelines carrying energy to western China. Rare earth elements in Kyrgyzstan and Tajikistan are also targets. The pattern mirrors Africa: infrastructure (pipelines, railways) for resource access.

## 5.5 The Resource Curse Dynamic — Structural Parallels

The **resource curse**—the paradox where countries with abundant natural resources tend to have less economic growth, worse development outcomes, and more conflict than countries with fewer resources—is well-documented. China's BRI extraction model intensifies several resource curse mechanisms:

| Resource Curse Mechanism | Colonial Era | BRI Era |
|--------------------------|--------------|---------|
| **Dutch disease** | Colonial exports crowded out local manufacturing | Resource exports to China crowd out local industrial development |
| **Enclave extraction** | Mines operated as isolated enclaves with minimal local linkage | Chinese industrial parks (e.g., IMIP in Indonesia) operate as enclaves with limited local spillover |
| **Corruption and rent-seeking** | Colonial concession companies bribed local elites | Chinese SOEs and local elites negotiate opaque contracts |
| **Conflict** | Resource extraction funded colonial wars and rebellions | Resource extraction in Myanmar, DRC funds armed groups |
| **Debt dependency** | Colonial indebtedness locked in extraction | BRI debt requires continued extraction to service loans |

High-value resources can "drive secessionist movements, provide start-up funds for rebel groups, and motivate government corruption". The DRC, Myanmar, and Nigeria all illustrate this dynamic.

**Counter-argument**: Some African leaders have openly welcomed Chinese investment precisely because it comes without the Western-style governance lectures (e.g., IMF conditionalities on democracy or human rights), framing it as a more respectful partnership. Furthermore, some scholars argue that the "curses" associated with extraction are "historically produced dynamic processes that need to be reevaluated in light of China's direct and indirect impacts". China's infrastructure investment *might* break the resource curse by providing roads, power, and ports that enable broader development. The evidence is mixed.

## 5.6 The Colonial Parallel — Concession Companies to Chinese SOEs

The 19th-century colonial extraction model was built on **concession companies**—private corporations granted exclusive rights to extract resources in exchange for payments to colonial powers. The Belgian Congo's *Force Publique*, the British South Africa Company (Cecil Rhodes), and the Dutch East India Company all operated on this model.

| Colonial Concession Company | BRI Equivalent |
|-----------------------------|-----------------|
| **British South Africa Company** (Rhodes) — minerals, land | Chinese SOEs (CMOC, Zijin, CNPC) — mining concessions across Africa |
| **Belgian Congo concession companies** — rubber, copper | China Molybdenum (Tenke Fungurume), Huayou Cobalt (DRC) |
| **Dutch East India Company (VOC)** — spices, trade | Chinese SOEs in Indonesia's nickel sector |
| **British Burma** — jade, rubies, teak | Chinese buyers of Myanmar's jade and rare earths |
| **United Fruit Company** — bananas, land in Latin America | Chinese lithium and copper acquisitions in Latin America |

**Key differences**:
- Colonial concession companies operated with **state-backed military force**; Chinese SOEs operate with contracts and diplomatic pressure.
- Colonial companies were often **private** (chartered by the state); Chinese SOEs are **state-owned**—the state and the corporation are the same entity.
- Colonial extraction was justified by **racial hierarchy** and "civilizing mission"; BRI extraction is justified by "win-win" and "South-South cooperation."

**Key similarity**: In both cases, resources flow out; profits flow to the metropolitan power; local populations bear environmental and social costs; and the extraction model creates dependency rather than diversified development.
