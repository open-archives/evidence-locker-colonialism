## Executive Summary

French colonial rule in Indochina (Vietnam, Laos, Cambodia) began in 1858 and intensified through rubber, rice, and opium extraction. After WWII, the U.S. replaced France as the primary external power, escalating military intervention to prevent a unified communist state. The resulting Vietnam War (1955–1975) caused ~3.8 million deaths, widespread chemical defoliation (Agent Orange), and a refugee diaspora that reshaped global migration. Post‑1975, economic embargoes and debt structures continued to constrain the region, while the U.S.‑backed Khmer Rouge in Cambodia (1975–1979) produced one of the deadliest genocides of the 20th century.

**Key Finding**: The Indochina case demonstrates the direct continuity from colonial resource extraction (rubber/opium) to Cold War proxy warfare, to modern neocolonial debt and infrastructure control.

## Part 1: Historical Timeline & Key Events

| Period | Event | Key Actors | Impact |
|--------|-------|------------|--------|
| **1858–1887** | French conquest of Cochinchina, Annam, Tonkin, Cambodia | France, Nguyen Dynasty | Creation of French Indochina (1887) |
| **1914–1940** | Rubber & opium monopolies; forced labour | French colonial administration | Economic extraction; resistance movements |
| **1940–1945** | Japanese occupation (Vichy France collaboration) | Japan, Vichy France | Weakened French control; rise of Viet Minh |
| **1945** | August Revolution; Ho Chi Minh declares independence | Viet Minh | France refuses recognition |
| **1946–1954** | First Indochina War (French vs. Viet Minh) | France, U.S. (funding 80% of French war costs) | French defeat at Dien Bien Phu |
| **1954** | Geneva Accords | France, Viet Minh, UK, USSR, USA, China | Vietnam temporarily divided at 17th parallel; Laos/Cambodia neutral |
| **1955–1963** | U.S. replaces France; supports Ngo Dinh Diem in South Vietnam | USA, South Vietnam | Diem regime; Buddhist crisis; U.S. military advisors |
| **1964–1973** | Direct U.S. combat intervention | USA, North Vietnam, Viet Cong | 2.7 million U.S. servicemen deployed; 58,220 U.S. deaths |
| **1965–1971** | Operation Ranch Hand (Agent Orange) | U.S. Air Force | 76 million litres of herbicide sprayed; 4.8 million Vietnamese exposed |
| **1973** | Paris Peace Accords | USA, North Vietnam | U.S. withdrawal; ceasefire violated |
| **1975** | Fall of Saigon | North Vietnamese Army | Unification; mass refugee exodus |
| **1975–1979** | Khmer Rouge genocide in Cambodia | Khmer Rouge (Pol Pot) | ~2 million deaths (25% of population) |
| **1976–1990s** | U.S. economic embargo; Vietnamese boat people | USA, Vietnam, ASEAN | ~800,000 refugees resettled abroad |

| **French colonial extraction focused on rubber, rice, and opium** | ✅ Yes | French colonial ledgers; academic economic histories | Opium monopoly was state‑sanctioned (not just black market) |

| **Boat people refugees faced international resettlement** | ✅ Yes | UNHCR statistics; refugee camp records | ~800,000 resettled; many died at sea |
| **Modern Vietnam is a net recipient of foreign investment (neocolonial)** | ⚠️ Partially | World Bank; IMF investment data | Rapid growth (GDP per capita 10x since 2000) but still dependent on export manufacturing for Western markets |

## Part 3: Key Colonial/Neocolonial Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Historical Form | Modern Equivalent |
|-----------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Rubber plantations** | Forced labour, land confiscation | Industrial plantations (Vietnam is now 3rd largest rubber exporter) |
| **Opium monopoly** | French state‑licensed production | Not applicable (opium illegal), but pharmaceutical supply chains resemble control |
| **Missionary schools** | French language/cultural erasure | English‑medium schools (post‑1975) + international NGOs |
| **Military bases** | French garrisons, then U.S. bases (Cam Ranh, Da Nang) | Russia leased Cam Ranh (1979–2002); now open to all (2016) |
| **Currency control** | Indochinese piastre (French colonial currency) | Vietnamese đồng (managed float); IMF structural adjustment after 1986 (Đổi Mới reforms) |
| **Debt dependency** | Post‑1975 loans from Soviet bloc, then Western banks | Vietnam’s external debt ~$140B (2024); major creditor: Japan, World Bank, China |
