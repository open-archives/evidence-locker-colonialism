## Executive Summary

The colonization of Indochina by France (1858–1954) established a system of resource extraction—primarily rubber, rice, and minerals—that relied on forced labor and the systematic suppression of indigenous rights. Following the French collapse, the United States escalated intervention to replace French institutions with a client state, eventually leading to prolonged warfare and the massive deployment of chemical weapons like Agent Orange. The legacy of this era includes enduring ecological devastation, a massive global refugee diaspora, and deep-seated political fragmentation.

**Key Finding**: The transition from French colonial extraction to U.S. Cold War intervention maintained a continuum of dependency and militarization that prioritize Western geopolitical interests over regional stability and indigenous autonomy.

## Part 3: Key Mechanisms of Control & Legacy

- **Colonial Exploitation (1858–1954):** France established a *colonie d'exploitation* focused on raw materials; the corvée system (forced labor) and heavy taxation financed colonial infrastructure built purely for French benefit.
- **The U.S. Client State (1954–1975):** Following the French defeat at Điện Biên Phủ, the U.S. bypassed the Geneva Accords to establish a dependent South Vietnamese state, viewing the region as a "domino" in global anti-communist strategy.
- **Ecological Warfare:** Over 80 million liters of herbicides (61% Agent Orange) were sprayed across 2.9 million hectares to destroy crops and jungle cover, leaving a permanent toxic footprint of dioxin in soil and the food chain.
- **Diaspora Fragmentation:** Post-1975 migration created significant diasporic communities in the U.S., Canada, France, and Australia, characterized by divided political memories and the trauma of exile.
