## Executive Summary

Residential schools were the most direct and brutal expression of colonial **cultural genocide** in North America. From the 1880s to the 1990s, the U.S. and Canadian governments — working through Christian churches (Catholic, Anglican, Presbyterian, United) — forcibly removed Indigenous children from their families, placed them in church-run boarding schools, and systematically erased their languages, cultures, and identities.

The goal was explicit: *"Kill the Indian in him, and save the man."* — Richard Henry Pratt, founder of Carlisle Indian School (1879).

In Canada, the **Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC)** documented over **150,000 children** forced into residential schools between 1883 and 1996. Over **4,100 deaths** were recorded — though the real number is likely much higher (the TRC estimate is "conservative" and excludes many unmarked graves). In the U.S., an estimated **100,000+ Indigenous children** attended federal and mission boarding schools from the 1870s to the 1960s.

**Key Finding:** Residential schools were not "education." They were **state-sanctioned cultural genocide** — designed to sever Indigenous children from their families, languages, and spiritual traditions. The trauma continues to affect Indigenous communities today through intergenerational PTSD, substance abuse, suicide, and family breakdown.

## Part 3: The "Indian Schools" — U.S. System

### Carlisle Indian School (1879–1918)

**Founder:** Richard Henry Pratt (U.S. Army officer)

**Motto:** *"Kill the Indian in him, and save the man."*

**Model:**
- Children forcibly removed from reservations
- Hair cut, given Western clothes, Christian names
- Forbidden to speak native languages
- Trained in trades (farming, carpentry) — not academics
- Military-style discipline; physical punishment

**Legacy:**
- Became the model for the entire U.S. boarding school system
- Over 300 schools operated across the U.S. by 1920
- Pratt's philosophy remains the foundation of U.S. assimilation policy

### U.S. System — Key Facts

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Total schools** | Over 300 (1879–1960s) |
| **Total children** | ~100,000+ (estimates vary) |
| **Peak enrollment** | ~30,000 (1920s) |
| **Church involvement** | Catholic, Protestant, Presbyterian, Methodist |
| **Official policy** | Assimilation (Dawes Act, 1887; Civilization Fund Act, 1819) |
| **Deaths recorded** | Thousands; no central tally |
| **Aftermath** | Some closure by 1970s; many transitioned to day schools |

**U.S. has not issued a formal apology.** Some states (like California) have issued their own.

## Part 5: The "Sixties Scoop" (Canada, 1960s–1980s)

### What Was It

The Sixties Scoop was a systematic removal of Indigenous children from their families by Canadian child welfare authorities — essentially a continuation of the residential school system under a different name.

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Duration** | 1960s–1980s (peaking in the 1970s) |
| **Number removed** | ~20,000 Indigenous children |
| **Method** | Social workers removed children from homes, placed in white foster families |
| **Justification** | "Child welfare" — but removal was often for poverty, cultural differences, not abuse |
| **Result** | Children grew up disconnected from their families, cultures, languages |
| **Apology** | 2019 (Jean Yves-Duclos, Canada's Minister of Families) |

**Quote from survivor:** *"We were stolen from our families — not because we were abused, but because we were Indian."*

## Part 6: Survivor Testimonies — The Human Cost

### Canada — TRC Testimonies

Over **6,000 survivors** testified before the TRC. Key themes:

| Theme | Example Quotes |
|-------|----------------|
| **Language erasure** | *"I was beaten for speaking Cree. They said it was the devil's language."* |
| **Physical abuse** | *"The nuns hit us with rulers. The priests were worse."* |
| **Sexual abuse** | *"The priest told me it was God's will. I didn't tell anyone for 40 years."* |
| **Starvation** | *"We were hungry all the time. We ate from the garbage."* |
| **Loss of culture** | *"I don't know how to be Anishinaabe. They took that from me."* |

### U.S. — Survivor Accounts

| Theme | Example |
|-------|---------|
| **Forced haircuts** | *"They cut our hair the first day. It was a punishment."* |
| **Names taken** | *"I was given a new name. They said my Indian name was 'uncivilized.'"* |
| **Religious conversion** | *"We were forced to pray to a God we didn't know."* |
| **Separation from siblings** | *"They took my brother away. I didn't see him for 10 years."* |

## Part 10: The Canadian Apology (2008)

**Prime Minister Stephen Harper (June 11, 2008):**

> *"The government of Canada... humbly apologizes to Aboriginal peoples for the fact that our country, which should have been a sanctuary for its peoples, instead created a system of education that was designed to destroy their cultures, languages, and identities."*

**What it did NOT include:**
- Financial compensation for all survivors
- Full accountability for the churches
- A plan for implementing all 94 TRC Calls to Action

**As of 2026, only ~50% of the TRC's calls to action have been fully implemented.**
