# Part 4: The Covert Empire — CIA Regime Change & Operation Condor

## 4.1 The Mechanism of Enforcement

If the 19th-century Monroe Doctrine relied on the British Royal Navy as a deterrent, and the early 20th-century Roosevelt Corollary relied on U.S. Marines and gunboats, the post-1945 era required a new enforcement mechanism. As overt colonialism collapsed and the Cold War escalated, direct military occupation became politically costly.

The solution was the **Central Intelligence Agency (CIA)**. Formed in 1947, the CIA became the primary enforcement arm of the Monroe Doctrine in the Western Hemisphere, utilizing covert action, economic sabotage, proxy paramilitaries, and state-sponsored terror to maintain U.S. hegemony.

## 4.2 Operation PBSUCCESS (Guatemala, 1954)

### The Threat
Jacobo Árbenz was democratically elected President of Guatemala in 1951. He initiated land reforms (Decree 900) that expropriated uncultivated land from large estates, most notably targeting the **United Fruit Company (UFCO)**, a powerful U.S. corporation with deep ties to the Eisenhower administration (CIA Director Allen Dulles and Secretary of State John Foster Dulles both had ties to UFCO's law firm).

### The Operation
In 1954, the CIA orchestrated **Operation PBSUCCESS**. 
- **Tactics**: Psychological warfare, radio propaganda, and funding a small proxy rebel force led by Carlos Castillo Armas.
- **Result**: Árbenz resigned and fled. Castillo Armas was installed as a dictator, reversing the land reforms and executing thousands of suspected dissidents.
- **Legacy**: PBSUCCESS became the CIA's blueprint for Latin American regime change. It proved that a government could be overthrown cheaply and covertly without deploying the U.S. military. It also sparked a 36-year civil war in Guatemala that resulted in over 200,000 deaths, primarily among Indigenous Maya.

## 4.3 Project FUBELT (Chile, 1973)

### The Threat
In 1970, Salvador Allende, a Marxist, was democratically elected President of Chile. He promised the nationalization of major industries, particularly U.S.-owned copper mines (Anaconda and Kennecott).

### The Operation
President Richard Nixon ordered the CIA to "make the economy scream." 
- **Track I**: Political funding and bribery to prevent Allende's confirmation by the Chilean Congress (failed).
- **Track II (Project FUBELT)**: Direct instigation of a military coup. The CIA funded anti-Allende propaganda (El Mercurio newspaper), orchestrated strikes by truck drivers to paralyze the economy, and supported right-wing military elements.
- **Result**: On September 11, 1973, General Augusto Pinochet led a violent military coup. Allende died in the presidential palace. Pinochet established a brutal dictatorship that killed or disappeared over 3,000 political opponents and tortured tens of thousands.

## 4.4 Operation Condor (1975–1980s)

### The Network
By the mid-1970s, the Southern Cone of South America (Chile, Argentina, Uruguay, Paraguay, Bolivia, and Brazil) was entirely controlled by right-wing military dictatorships. These regimes, facing left-wing insurgencies and political opposition, established **Operation Condor** in 1975 — a transnational intelligence and assassination network.

### U.S. Involvement
Declassified documents (including the CIA's own internal reports and the "Terror Archive" discovered in Paraguay in 1992) reveal that the U.S. government provided:
- **Communications infrastructure**: A secure communications system out of the U.S. military base in the Panama Canal Zone.
- **Funding and technical support**: Surveillance equipment and training.
- **Tacit approval**: Secretary of State Henry Kissinger explicitly told Argentine military leaders in 1976: *"If there are things that have to be done, you should do them quickly."*

### The Toll
Operation Condor resulted in an estimated **60,000 to 80,000 deaths**, largely of left-wing activists, students, labor leaders, and intellectuals, many of whom were kidnapped, tortured, and dropped from helicopters into the ocean ("death flights").

## 4.5 The School of the Americas (WHINSEC)

The ideological and tactical foundation for this covert empire was the **U.S. Army School of the Americas (SOA)**, located in Panama and later moved to Fort Benning, Georgia. 

- **Purpose**: To train Latin American military and police personnel in anti-communist counter-insurgency.
- **The Curriculum**: In 1996, the Pentagon was forced to declassify training manuals used at the SOA, which explicitly taught execution, extortion, torture, and blackmail.
- **The Alumni**: The school trained over 60,000 Latin American soldiers. Its graduates included some of the hemisphere's most notorious dictators and human rights abusers, including Manuel Noriega (Panama), Leopoldo Galtieri (Argentina), and the founders of the Los Zetas cartel (Mexico).

## 4.6 The Dirty Wars and the Contras (1980s)

During the Reagan administration, the Monroe Doctrine's covert application shifted to Central America.

### Nicaragua and the Contras
Following the 1979 Sandinista revolution in Nicaragua, the CIA funded and trained the **Contras**, a right-wing rebel group, to overthrow the Sandinista government.
- When Congress banned funding for the Contras (the Boland Amendment), the Reagan administration illegally sold arms to Iran and funneled the profits to the Contras (The Iran-Contra Affair).
- Simultaneously, elements of the CIA turned a blind eye to Contra involvement in cocaine trafficking to fund their war, fueling the 1980s U.S. crack epidemic. *(See main repo: `EL-CONTRA-COCAINE-1980s`)*.

### El Salvador and Honduras
The U.S. provided billions in military aid to the right-wing government of El Salvador during its civil war, supporting military units (like the Atlacatl Battalion, trained at the SOA) that committed atrocities such as the **El Mozote massacre** (where over 800 civilians were executed). In Honduras, the CIA trained Battalion 316, a death squad that disappeared hundreds of political opponents.
