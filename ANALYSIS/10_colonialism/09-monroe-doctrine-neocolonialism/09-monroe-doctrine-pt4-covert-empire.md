# Part 4: The Covert Empire — CIA Regime Change, Operation Condor, and the Institutionalization of the Monroe Doctrine

## 4.1 Context — From "Good Neighbor" to "Covert Action"

The Good Neighbor Policy (1933–1945) had genuinely reduced overt U.S. military interventions in Latin America. But the end of World War II and the onset of the Cold War transformed the geopolitical landscape. The United States, now in a global struggle with the Soviet Union, viewed Latin America as a critical theater. Any government perceived as "communist" or "unstable" was a potential Soviet foothold — and a threat to U.S. security.

**The shift was dramatic**:

| Era | Mechanism | Examples |
|-----|-----------|----------|
| **Monroe Doctrine (1823–1904)** | Rhetorical claim; no enforcement capacity | U.S. too weak to act |
| **Roosevelt Corollary (1904–1933)** | Gunboat diplomacy; direct military intervention | Banana Wars; customs receiverships |
| **Good Neighbor Policy (1933–1945)** | Rhetorical non-intervention; economic control | Withdrawal of troops; corporate dominance |
| **Cold War (1947–1991)** | **Covert action; CIA regime change** | Guatemala, Chile, Operation Condor, Contra War |
| **Post-Cold War (1991–present)** | Drug war; neoliberal economics; competition with China | Plan Colombia; critical minerals competition |

**Overt vs. covert coexistence**: **Covert action coexisted with overt military interventions during the Cold War (e.g., Dominican Republic 1965, Grenada 1983, Panama 1989 — covered in Part 5). The U.S. used both tools in parallel, choosing covert action when deniability was needed and overt intervention when the political stakes demanded a show of force or when covert action had failed.**

**The institutional shift**: The creation of the Central Intelligence Agency (CIA) in 1947 provided the United States with a new tool — covert action. The CIA could overthrow governments, assassinate leaders, and wage proxy wars without the political cost of overt military intervention. The Monroe Doctrine was now enforced by intelligence officers, not Marines.

---

## 4.2 The CIA as Monroe Doctrine Enforcement Arm

The CIA's mission in Latin America was framed as "anti-communist" and "hemispheric security." But in practice, it was the institutionalized continuation of the Monroe Doctrine — the United States asserting its unilateral right to determine the political orientation of the hemisphere.

**Key mechanisms**:

| Mechanism | Description | Examples |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Regime change** | Covert operations to overthrow governments | Guatemala (1954), Chile (1973), Guyana (1960s) |
| **Proxy warfare** | Funding and training anti-government forces | Nicaragua Contras, El Salvador civil war |
| **Economic sabotage** | Destabilizing economies to create conditions for coup | Chile (Allende), Guatemala (Arbenz) |
| **Political influence** | Funding political parties, media, and labor unions | Brazil, Argentina, Venezuela |
| **State terror networks** | Supporting transnational intelligence and assassination operations | Operation Condor |
| **Training and doctrine** | Teaching counterinsurgency and repression to Latin American militaries | School of the Americas |

**The continuity**: The CIA did not invent these mechanisms. The Roosevelt Corollary had already established the principle of U.S. intervention. The CIA simply operationalized it — replacing gunboats with paramilitaries, invasions with intelligence operations.

## 4.3 Operation PBSUCCESS (1954) — The Blueprint for CIA Regime Change

**Guatemala, 1954** — The first successful CIA regime change in Latin America. It became the blueprint for decades of covert operations.

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Target** | President Jacobo Árbenz (Guatemala) |
| **Context** | Árbenz had initiated land reform, expropriating unused land from United Fruit Company; his government legalized the communist party and accepted Soviet weapons |
| **U.S. interest** | United Fruit (U.S. corporation) lobbied heavily; Cold War anti-communism provided ideological cover |
| **CIA operation** | PBSUCCESS — trained and funded a paramilitary force led by exiled Colonel Carlos Castillo Armas; conducted psychological warfare (radio broadcasts, leaflets); bribed Guatemalan military officers |
| **Outcome** | Árbenz resigned in June 1954; Castillo Armas took power; land reforms were reversed; Guatemala entered decades of civil war and repression |
| **Legacy** | PBSUCCESS established the CIA template: paramilitary force + psychological warfare + military bribery + diplomatic pressure |

**The colonial echo**: The United Fruit Company operated as a corporate colonial power in Central America — a 20th-century version of the British East India Company. The U.S. government intervened to protect corporate interests, just as European colonial powers had intervened to protect their chartered companies.

## 4.4 Project FUBELT (1973) — The Overthrow of Salvador Allende

**Chile, 1973** — A more sophisticated CIA operation that demonstrated the agency's full toolkit.

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Target** | President Salvador Allende (Chile) |
| **Context** | Allende, a socialist, was elected in 1970; he nationalized copper mines (U.S. corporate interests); he established diplomatic relations with Cuba |
| **U.S. interest** | Economic (copper, U.S. corporate holdings); ideological (Cold War anti-communism); geopolitical (fear of Soviet influence in South America) |
| **CIA operation** | Project FUBELT — funding and support for opposition parties, media, and labor unions; economic sabotage (pressuring banks, stopping credit); propaganda; political assassination (including General René Schneider, a pro-constitutional military figure); and ultimately support for military coup |
| **Outcome** | September 11, 1973 — military coup led by General Augusto Pinochet; Allende died (suicide or assassinated); Pinochet established a brutal military dictatorship |
| **Legacy** | Pinochet regime (1973–1990) killed thousands, tortured thousands, and displaced millions; it became a model for other Latin American dictatorships; the CIA's role was documented in declassified U.S. records |

**The colonial echo**: The U.S. overthrew a democratically elected government to protect corporate interests and ideological alignment — a pattern that repeated across Latin America. The economic sabotage (cutting credit, manipulating copper prices) was the financial equivalent of gunboat diplomacy.

## 4.5 Operation Condor (1970s–1980s) — The Transnational Terror Network

**Operation Condor** was a secret intelligence-sharing network among the military dictatorships of the Southern Cone — Argentina, Bolivia, Brazil, Chile, Paraguay, and Uruguay — with active U.S. support (CIA, State Department, and Pentagon). It is the most extreme example of U.S.-backed state terror in the hemisphere.

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Origins** | 1975 — formalized by the intelligence chiefs of the participating countries in Santiago, Chile |
| **Scope** | Cross-border surveillance, torture, kidnappings, assassinations, and "disappearances" of political dissidents across national borders |
| **Methods** | Intelligence sharing; joint operations; extrajudicial killings; torture and forced disappearance |
| **Scale** | **Tens of thousands killed or "disappeared" across the region (estimates vary: Argentina commonly cited as 30,000; Chile ~3,000; Uruguay ~200; Bolivia/Paraguay/Brazil thousands)** |
| **U.S. role** | CIA intelligence sharing; technical support; training (School of the Americas); diplomatic cover; use of U.S. military bases and communications infrastructure |
| **Notable victims** | Orlando Letelier (Chilean diplomat assassinated in Washington D.C., 1976 — the most high-profile Condor killing on U.S. soil) |
| **Legacy** | Operation Condor was a transnational state terror network — the first such network in the Western Hemisphere; it set the template for later regional counterterrorism operations |

**U.S. distance in the 1980s**: **As human rights became a more significant factor in U.S. foreign policy (particularly under the Carter administration and later Congressional oversight), the U.S. began to distance itself from the worst abuses. The Intelligence Oversight Act (1980) and other reforms constrained CIA operations, though support for anti-communist regimes continued through other channels.**

**The colonial echo**: Operation Condor was a 20th-century version of colonial intelligence networks — the British Empire's "Great Game" in Asia, the French colonial intelligence networks in Africa. It was an alliance of colonial-style states (the military dictatorships) with the metropolitan power (the U.S.) to suppress dissent across the hemisphere.

## 4.6 The School of the Americas — Training the Enforcers

The **School of the Americas (SOA)** was a U.S. military training facility for Latin American military personnel, established in 1946 in Panama and later moved to Fort Benning, Georgia. It provided counterinsurgency training, intelligence techniques, and "psychological operations" — tools that were used in brutal repression.

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Graduates** | Over 60,000 Latin American military and police personnel trained |
| **Notable graduates** | Manuel Noriega (Panama), Leopoldo Galtieri (Argentina), Oscar Mejía Víctores (Guatemala) — all later accused of human rights abuses |
| **Curriculum** | Counterinsurgency, intelligence gathering, interrogation techniques, psychological operations; later revealed to include teachings on torture and repression |
| **Known abuses** | SOA graduates were implicated in the El Mozote massacre (El Salvador), the Guatemala genocide, and Operation Condor |
| **Legacy** | The SOA was renamed the "Western Hemisphere Institute for Security Cooperation" (WHINSEC) in 2001, but the mission and curriculum largely continued |

**The colonial echo**: The School of the Americas trained the enforcers of the Monroe Doctrine — the military officers who suppressed dissent, protected corporate interests, and served as the regional allies of the U.S. It was the institutionalization of the Roosevelt Corollary's "police power."

## 4.7 The Dirty Wars — Argentina, El Salvador, Honduras, and Beyond

The U.S. supported multiple Latin American regimes engaged in brutal repression:

| Country | Period | U.S. Support | Outcome |
|---------|--------|--------------|---------|
| **Argentina** | 1976–1983 (military junta) | CIA intelligence, training, military aid | "Dirty War" — 30,000 disappeared; U.S. supported junta diplomatically and militarily |
| **El Salvador** | 1979–1992 (civil war) | Massive military aid ($6 billion+); training; SOE | Government forces (including SOA graduates) committed atrocities (El Mozote massacre); U.S. supported the Salvadoran government |
| **Honduras** | 1980s | U.S. military base (Palmerola); support for Contra camps | Honduras served as a base for Contra operations against Nicaragua; U.S. presence destabilized the region |
| **Guatemala** | 1960–1996 (civil war) | CIA, military aid, SOA training | Genocide against the Maya (including Ríos Montt, SOA graduate); U.S. supported successive military governments |
| **Nicaragua** | 1980s (Contra War) | CIA funding and training of Contra rebels | A proxy war against the Sandinista government; U.S. violated international law (ICJ ruling) |

**The colonial echo**: The Dirty Wars were a 20th-century version of colonial repression — the suppression of dissent through state terror, justified by anti-communism and "stability." The U.S. was not the direct perpetrator in many cases, but it was the enabler, the trainer, and the strategic backer.

## 4.8 Alternative Perspectives — Balancing the Analysis

### Cold War Realist Perspective
- The U.S. was in a global struggle with the Soviet Union. Cuba (1959) was a Soviet ally; Nicaragua (1979) had Soviet/Cuban ties. The U.S. feared a "domino effect" in the hemisphere.
- The CIA operations were defensive — preventing Soviet expansion, protecting U.S. security interests, and ensuring access to strategic resources (oil, copper, shipping lanes).
- The human rights costs were not the intended outcome; they were collateral damage in a necessary geopolitical struggle. **Many operations had bipartisan support in Congress initially.**

### Latin American Right-Wing Perspective
- Some Latin American elites welcomed U.S. support as protection against socialist revolution. They feared land reform, nationalization, and political upheaval.
- The military regimes were seen by some as necessary to maintain order and economic development. **Under Pinochet, the Chicago Boys implemented neoliberal reforms that stabilized the economy, though at severe human cost.**

### Latin American Resistance Perspective
- The U.S. overthrew democracies, supported dictatorships, and violated sovereignty. The Latin American left viewed this as imperialism — the Monroe Doctrine in its most brutal form.
- Organizations like the Chilean resistance, the Sandinistas, and the Salvadoran guerrillas fought against U.S.-backed regimes. They saw the U.S. as the primary obstacle to liberation.

### U.S. Domestic Critique
- The CIA operations were secret and often violated U.S. law and values. Congressional investigations (e.g., Church Committee, 1975) exposed many abuses.
- **Post-1970s reforms (e.g., intelligence oversight committees, the Hughes-Ryan Act) were a direct response to these abuses, constraining covert action and requiring presidential approval and congressional notification.**
- Critics argued that the U.S. had betrayed its own principles — democracy, human rights, and self-determination — in the name of anti-communism.

### Mixed Long-Term Outcomes
- Some regimes transitioned to democracy in the 1980s–90s (e.g., Chile, Argentina). Civil wars ended, but legacies of trauma, inequality, and distrust persist.
- The Cold War's end shifted U.S. policy away from anti-communist intervention toward drug interdiction and neoliberal economic tools — a new phase of the Monroe Doctrine.

## 4.9 The Colonial Continuum — From Monroe to Covert Empire

| Era | Mechanism | Colonial Echo |
|-----|-----------|---------------|
| **Monroe Doctrine (1823)** | Rhetorical claim | European colonialism excluded |
| **Roosevelt Corollary (1904)** | Gunboat diplomacy; direct intervention | British gunboat diplomacy in China |
| **Good Neighbor (1933)** | Rhetorical non-intervention; economic control | Informal empire; corporate dominance |
| **Covert Empire (1947–1991)** | CIA regime change; state terror | British East India Company; colonial intelligence networks |
| **War on Drugs (1990s–present)** | Militarized counter-narcotics; regional security | Resource extraction; dependency |
