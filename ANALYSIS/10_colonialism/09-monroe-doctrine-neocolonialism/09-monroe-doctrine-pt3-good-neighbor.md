# Part 3: The Good Neighbor Policy (1933) — Rhetoric vs. Reality

## 3.1 Context — The Retreat from Direct Occupation

By the early 1930s, the "international police power" asserted by the Roosevelt Corollary had become increasingly unsustainable for the United States. Decades of "Banana Wars" and military occupations across the Caribbean and Central America (Nicaragua, Haiti, Dominican Republic) had yielded significant blowback.

**Key Drivers for Policy Change:**
1. **The Great Depression (1929):** The U.S. economy collapsed, making the financial and military costs of occupying foreign nations politically unpopular.
2. **Rising Anti-Americanism:** Decades of Marine occupations had galvanized Latin American nationalism and unified regional opposition against "Yankee Imperialism."
3. **The Sandino Rebellion:** In Nicaragua, a protracted guerrilla war led by Augusto César Sandino bogged down U.S. Marines from 1927 to 1933, proving that direct military occupation could be fiercely and effectively resisted.
4. **European Fascism:** As authoritarian regimes rose in Europe, the U.S. recognized a strategic need to solidify hemispheric solidarity rather than alienate its neighbors.

---

## 3.2 The Policy Shift — FDR's "Good Neighbor"

In his 1933 inaugural address, President Franklin D. Roosevelt announced a rhetorical shift:

> *"In the field of world policy I would dedicate this Nation to the policy of the good neighbor—the neighbor who resolutely respects himself and, because he does so, respects the rights of others."*

Later that year, at the **Montevideo Convention on the Rights and Duties of States (1933)**, Secretary of State Cordell Hull signed a treaty stating that *"No state has the right to intervene in the internal or external affairs of another."*

This legally and rhetorically repudiated the Roosevelt Corollary. 

### Tangible Actions
- **Withdrawal of Marines:** U.S. troops were withdrawn from Nicaragua (1933) and Haiti (1934).
- **Abrogation of the Platt Amendment:** In 1934, the U.S. formally ended the amendment that had legalized its right to intervene in Cuban affairs.
- **Respect for Nationalization (Mexico, 1938):** When Mexico nationalized its oil industry (creating PEMEX), FDR resisted calls from U.S. oil companies to intervene militarily, opting instead for a negotiated financial settlement.

## 3.3 The Reality — Outsourcing the Empire

While the direct military occupations ended, the Monroe Doctrine's fundamental goal—maintaining the Western Hemisphere as an uncontested U.S. sphere of influence—did not change. Instead of deploying U.S. Marines, the Good Neighbor Policy relied on **proxy strongmen** and **economic hegemony**.

### 3.3.1 The Rise of the Dictators
Before leaving countries like Nicaragua and the Dominican Republic, the U.S. military trained and equipped local constabulary forces (National Guards). These U.S.-trained forces were left in the hands of loyal strongmen who maintained "order" and protected U.S. corporate interests (like the United Fruit Company) without requiring a U.S. troop presence.

| Country | The Proxy Strongman | Mechanism of Control |
|---------|---------------------|----------------------|
| **Nicaragua** | Anastasio Somoza García | Head of the U.S.-created National Guard; assassinated Sandino; founded a brutal dynasty that ruled until 1979. |
| **Dominican Republic** | Rafael Trujillo | Rose through the U.S.-created Constabulary Guard; established one of the hemisphere's bloodiest dictatorships (1930–1961). |
| **Cuba** | Fulgencio Batista | Supported by the U.S. to maintain stability after the Platt Amendment's repeal; protected U.S. sugar and casino interests. |

*(FDR is apocryphally quoted regarding Somoza or Trujillo: "He may be a son of a bitch, but he's our son of a bitch.")*

### 3.3.2 Economic Integration
Cordell Hull spearheaded the **Reciprocal Trade Agreements Act (1934)**. While framed as mutually beneficial, these agreements lowered tariffs in ways that structurally favored U.S. manufactured goods while locking Latin American economies into exporting cheap raw materials and agricultural products. It solidified a dependency model that persisted throughout the 20th century.
