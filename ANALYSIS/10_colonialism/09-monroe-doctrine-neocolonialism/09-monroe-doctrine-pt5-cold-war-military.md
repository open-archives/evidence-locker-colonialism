# Part 5: Cold War Interventions — Direct Military Action

---

## 5.1 Context — Covert vs. Overt

Part 4 examined the CIA's covert empire — regime change, state terror, and proxy warfare. But the United States also continued to use **overt military force** in Latin America during the Cold War. These direct interventions were less frequent than covert operations but were politically significant — they demonstrated that the U.S. would still use its military power when covert action was insufficient or when the political stakes demanded a show of force.

**The key distinction**:

| Dimension | Covert Operations (Part 4) | Direct Military Action (Part 5) |
|-----------|---------------------------|--------------------------------|
| **Visibility** | Secret; deniable | Public; acknowledged |
| **Mechanism** | CIA, paramilitaries, proxies | U.S. Marines, Navy, Air Force |
| **Justification** | Anti-communist; hemispheric security | Humanitarian; anti-communist; stability |
| **Political cost** | Lower; deniable | Higher; public accountability |
| **Examples** | Guatemala (1954), Chile (1973), Condor | Bay of Pigs (1961), Dominican Republic (1965), Grenada (1983), Panama (1989) |

**The continuity**: Direct military action was the overt continuation of the Roosevelt Corollary's "police power." The U.S. still asserted the right to intervene in the hemisphere — but by the Cold War, it did so in the name of anti-communism rather than "civilization."

**Covert vs. overt coexistence**: Covert action coexisted with overt military interventions during the Cold War. The U.S. used both tools in parallel, choosing covert action when deniability was needed and overt intervention when the political stakes demanded a show of force or when covert action had failed. Direct interventions became rarer as covert and proxy models matured, but overt force remained an option throughout the Cold War and beyond.

## 5.2 Bay of Pigs (1961) — The Failed Invasion

The Bay of Pigs invasion was a failed CIA-organized paramilitary invasion of Cuba, launched in April 1961. It was a direct military action — though covertly planned — and it was a catastrophic failure.

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Context** | Fidel Castro had overthrown the U.S.-backed Batista regime in 1959; Cuba established diplomatic and military ties with the Soviet Union; U.S. interests (sugar, property) were expropriated |
| **Plan** | CIA-trained Cuban exiles (Brigade 2506) would land at the Bay of Pigs, establish a beachhead, and rally popular support against Castro |
| **U.S. role** | CIA planning and funding; U.S. Navy and Air Force provided logistical support (but no direct combat); President Kennedy's decision not to commit full air support was controversial |
| **Outcome** | Total failure — Cuban forces defeated the exiles in 72 hours; ~1,200 exiles were captured; the U.S. was humiliated internationally |
| **Legacy** | The Bay of Pigs was a strategic disaster; it strengthened Castro's position; it pushed Cuba closer to the Soviet Union (leading to the Cuban Missile Crisis, 1962); it led to a reevaluation of U.S. covert operations |

**The colonial echo**: The Bay of Pigs was a 20th-century version of the "gunboat diplomacy" that had characterized the Roosevelt Corollary — but executed incompetently. It demonstrated that the U.S. could no longer simply impose its will in the hemisphere.

## 5.3 Dominican Republic (1965) — The First Overt Intervention of the Cold War

In April 1965, the U.S. invaded the Dominican Republic to prevent a potential communist takeover. It was the first overt U.S. military intervention in Latin America in the Cold War era.

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Context** | Civil war erupted in the Dominican Republic between "constitutionalists" (supporters of ousted President Juan Bosch, a left-leaning democrat) and "loyalists" (military rebels who opposed Bosch's return). The U.S. feared a "second Cuba" if leftist forces gained control |
| **U.S. response** | President Lyndon B. Johnson ordered Operation Power Pack — the invasion of the Dominican Republic with ~23,000 U.S. troops |
| **Official justification** | Protecting U.S. citizens; preventing the spread of communism; restoring order |
| **Outcome** | U.S. forces secured the country; a ceasefire was negotiated; elections were held in 1966 (Joaquín Balaguer won, backed by the U.S.) |
| **Legacy** | The intervention succeeded in preventing a leftist takeover in the short term, but it deepened anti-American sentiment; it demonstrated that the U.S. would use overt military force to prevent communist expansion in the hemisphere; it was controversial in the U.S. and criticized by Latin American nations |

**The colonial echo**: The Dominican Republic intervention was a direct application of the Roosevelt Corollary — the U.S. acting as the "international police power" in the hemisphere. It was justified not in the language of "civilization" but in the language of anti-communism and hemispheric stability.

## 5.4 Grenada (1983) — Operation Urgent Fury

In October 1983, the U.S. invaded the small Caribbean island of Grenada. It was the first major U.S. military intervention since Vietnam and was widely seen as a reassertion of U.S. power in the hemisphere.

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Context** | Grenada had gained independence from Britain in 1974; a Marxist-Leninist government led by Maurice Bishop took power in 1979; in October 1983, a hardline faction within the government assassinated Bishop and took control |
| **U.S. response** | President Ronald Reagan ordered Operation Urgent Fury — the invasion of Grenada with ~7,600 U.S. troops |
| **Official justification** | Protecting U.S. medical students (800+ were on the island); preventing a Cuban-Soviet foothold; restoring order; responding to the assassination of Bishop |
| **Outcome** | U.S. forces quickly secured the island; the Cuban-Soviet presence was eliminated; elections were held in 1984; the invasion was widely supported domestically but condemned by many Latin American and European nations |
| **Legacy** | Grenada was a quick military success; it demonstrated U.S. willingness to use force in the hemisphere; it was seen as a reassertion of the Monroe Doctrine; it was the first time the U.S. had invaded a Caribbean nation since the Dominican Republic (1965) |

**The colonial echo**: Grenada was a 20th-century version of the "police power" established by the Roosevelt Corollary — the U.S. intervening unilaterally to prevent a leftist government in its sphere of influence. The justification was updated: humanitarian (rescuing students) and anti-communist.

## 5.5 Panama (1989) — Operation Just Cause

In December 1989, the U.S. invaded Panama to remove General Manuel Noriega, a former CIA asset turned drug trafficker. It was the largest U.S. military intervention in Latin America since the Vietnam War and the first post-Cold War intervention in the hemisphere.

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Context** | Manuel Noriega, Panama's de facto leader, had been a U.S. intelligence asset for years but had become a liability — he was implicated in drug trafficking, money laundering, and human rights abuses; he had also increasingly defied U.S. demands |
| **U.S. response** | President George H.W. Bush ordered Operation Just Cause — the invasion of Panama with ~27,000 U.S. troops |
| **Official justification** | Protecting U.S. citizens (Noriega had declared a state of war with the U.S.); combating drug trafficking; restoring democracy; enforcing U.S. treaty rights (the Panama Canal) |
| **Outcome** | U.S. forces quickly defeated Panamanian forces; Noriega surrendered and was captured; he was taken to the U.S. for trial and convicted on drug trafficking charges; thousands of Panamanian civilians were displaced and hundreds were killed (estimates vary widely; civilian casualties were significant) |
| **Legacy** | The invasion was successful in removing Noriega but caused lasting resentment in Panama and Latin America; it was condemned internationally as a violation of sovereignty; it was the last major U.S. military intervention in Latin America of the Cold War era |

**The colonial echo**: Panama was a complex mix of Monroe Doctrine (sphere-of-influence enforcement), drug war policy, and post-Cold War democracy promotion. It demonstrated the continuity of U.S. unilateralism — the assertion that the U.S. could intervene in the hemisphere whenever it deemed necessary, even when the justification had shifted from anti-communism to the war on drugs.

## 5.7 Alternative Perspectives — Balancing the Analysis

### Realist/Anti-Communist View
- Interventions responded to real Soviet and Cuban expansion (arms, advisors, training revolutionaries). The U.S. was defending itself and its allies.
- Dominican Republic (1965) succeeded in preventing an immediate leftist takeover and led to elections (Joaquín Balaguer won). Grenada (1983) was a quick success and domestically popular. Panama (1989) removed a drug trafficking dictator.
- The U.S. had legitimate security interests, including the Panama Canal, and the right to protect U.S. citizens.

### Sovereignty Critique
- Each intervention violated the non-intervention principles established in the Good Neighbor Policy and the OAS Charter. The U.S. repeatedly asserted unilateral control over hemispheric affairs.
- Civilian casualties (particularly in Panama) were significant; the U.S. inflicted harm on sovereign states to achieve its objectives.
- The interventions set dangerous precedents: the U.S. could invade any country it deemed threatening.

### Democracy Promotion vs. Hypocrisy
- Interventions were sometimes framed as "restoring democracy" — e.g., Panama, Grenada, Dominican Republic — but the U.S. had supported dictatorships elsewhere (e.g., Pinochet in Chile, the military junta in Argentina).
- The selective application of democracy promotion undermined the credibility of U.S. rhetoric.

### Domestic U.S. Politics
- Interventions were often popular domestically, especially Grenada (1983) and Panama (1989), where quick victories boosted presidential approval.
- However, there was opposition from anti-imperialist and progressive voices in the U.S. who saw the interventions as violations of international law and U.S. values.

### Latin American Reaction
- Interventions deepened anti-American sentiment across the region. The invasions were seen as evidence that the U.S. had not truly abandoned gunboat diplomacy.
- They fueled support for leftist movements and organizations that resisted U.S. intervention, such as the Sandinistas in Nicaragua and various guerilla groups.

## 5.8 The Colonial Continuum — From Monroe to Direct Action

The direct military interventions of the Cold War era represent the overt continuation of the Roosevelt Corollary's "police power":

| Era | Mechanism | Colonial Echo |
|-----|-----------|---------------|
| **Monroe Doctrine (1823)** | Rhetorical claim | European colonialism excluded |
| **Roosevelt Corollary (1904)** | Gunboat diplomacy; direct intervention | British gunboat diplomacy in China |
| **Good Neighbor (1933)** | Rhetorical non-intervention; economic control | Informal empire; corporate dominance |
| **Covert Empire (1947–1991)** | CIA regime change; state terror | British East India Company; colonial intelligence networks |
| **Direct Action (1961–1989)** | Overt military intervention | Gunboat diplomacy; colonial "police actions" |
| **War on Drugs (1990s–present)** | Militarized counter-narcotics; regional security | Resource extraction; dependency |

**The continuity**: The direct interventions of the Cold War were a reassertion of the Roosevelt Corollary — the U.S. acting as the "international police power" in the hemisphere. The justifications evolved (anti-communism, drug trafficking, democracy promotion), but the logic remained the same: the U.S. would intervene unilaterally whenever it deemed necessary.
