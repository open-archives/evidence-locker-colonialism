## Series Abstract

For two centuries, the Monroe Doctrine has been the foundation of U.S. policy in the Western Hemisphere — a unilateral assertion that the Americas are a U.S. sphere of influence, closed to extra-hemispheric powers. First directed at European colonialism, then at Soviet communism, the doctrine has been enforced through gunboat diplomacy, covert action, economic coercion, and military intervention.

This 9-part series traces the evolution of the Monroe Doctrine from its 1823 origins to the present-day competition with China. It examines the mechanisms of U.S. control — from the Roosevelt Corollary's "international police power" to the CIA's covert empire, from the War on Drugs to neoliberal structural adjustment — and the resistance they have provoked.

The series argues that the Monroe Doctrine is not dead. It has been transformed from a unilateral U.S. assertion of control to a contested framework in a multipolar world. China's economic presence has created genuine alternatives for Latin American countries, and they are using that leverage to pursue their own interests. The Western Hemisphere is no longer a U.S. "backyard." It is a theater of great-power competition, where Latin American countries are increasingly the actors, not the objects.

**The central question**: Will the U.S. adapt to this new reality — offering positive alternatives, respecting Latin American sovereignty, and building genuine partnerships? Or will it cling to the old logic of unilateral control, treating cooperation as a threat and alienating its neighbors?
