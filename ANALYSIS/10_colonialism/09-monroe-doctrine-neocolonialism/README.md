## Entry 1: The Original Doctrine (1823)

### What It Was

On December 2, 1823, President James Monroe delivered his annual message to Congress, declaring that the Western Hemisphere was closed to further European colonization. The doctrine made four basic points:

| Principle | Content |
|-----------|---------|
| **1** | The U.S. would not interfere in European wars or internal affairs |
| **2** | The U.S. recognized and would not interfere with existing European colonies |
| **3** | The Western Hemisphere was closed to future colonization |
| **4** | Any European attempt to control a nation in the Americas would be viewed as a hostile act against the U.S. |

### The Context

Monroe was concerned about two things: Russia's territorial ambitions on the Northwest coast of North America, and European designs on the newly independent republics of Latin America. British Foreign Secretary George Canning had suggested a joint U.S.-British declaration, but Monroe's Secretary of State John Quincy Adams convinced him to make it a unilateral American statement.

### The Audacity

In 1823, the U.S. had virtually no standing army and only a small navy of five sloops of war. European powers never recognized the doctrine as a principle of international law—it was, and remains, a unilateral declaration.

> *"The European powers, even those that became allies of the U.S., never accorded the Doctrine recognition. To this day it has no standing as a principle of international law."*

### The Deterrent Effect

Despite its lack of legal force, the doctrine was remarkably successful. Since Monroe enunciated it, not a single Latin American state has lost its independence as a result of outright aggression from outside the hemisphere. The only exceptions occurred during the U.S. Civil War—Spain's brief re-annexation of Santo Domingo (1861–1865) and France's installation of Maximilian as Emperor of Mexico (1863–1867)—both of which ended after the U.S. reasserted itself.

**But success had a cost.** The original concept of "hands off" to Europe was eventually stretched to allow "hands on" for the United States.

## Entry 2: The Roosevelt Corollary (1904)

### The Shift

In 1904, President Theodore Roosevelt dramatically expanded the Monroe Doctrine. The original doctrine was essentially passive—it asked Europeans not to increase their influence. Roosevelt transformed it into an active justification for U.S. intervention.

### The Language

In his annual message to Congress in December 1904, Roosevelt declared:

> *"Chronic wrongdoing... may in America, as elsewhere, ultimately require intervention by some civilized nation, and in the Western Hemisphere the adherence of the United States to the Monroe Doctrine may force the United States, however reluctantly, in flagrant cases of such wrongdoing or impotence, to the exercise of an international police power."*

### The Irony

The corollary contained a great irony: the Monroe Doctrine had been sought to prevent European intervention in the Western Hemisphere, but the Roosevelt Corollary justified American intervention throughout the Western Hemisphere.

Roosevelt's policy became known as "Big Stick" diplomacy. The United States would now act as a "regional policeman," intervening in Cuba, Nicaragua, Haiti, and the Dominican Republic.

## Entry 3: Cold War Interventions (1954–1989)

### The Cold War Expansion

By the 1950s, U.S. policymakers were using the Monroe Doctrine as a powerful legitimizer for paramilitary interventions. The doctrine was expanded to justify intervention to prevent any state in the Americas from becoming what the U.S. regarded as communist.

### Major Interventions

| Country | Year | Action |
|---------|------|--------|
| **Guatemala** | 1954 | CIA-led coup overthrew democratically elected president Jacobo Árbenz |
| **Cuba** | 1961 | Bay of Pigs invasion; ongoing embargo |
| **Dominican Republic** | 1965 | U.S. sent 40,000 troops to "restore order" after civil war |
| **Chile** | 1973 | CIA-backed coup overthrew Salvador Allende |
| **Grenada** | 1983 | U.S. invasion |
| **Panama** | 1989 | U.S. invasion; overthrow of Manuel Noriega |

### The Pattern

The Monroe Doctrine projected its silhouette into contemporary history through these invasions. Its approach promotes, argues, fabricates, and makes credible perceptions of threats to national security in Latin America.

## Entry 4: The "Banana Republic" Era

### Economic Exploitation

In 1904, American writer O. Henry coined the term "banana republic" to describe countries under the control of U.S. capital, whose economies depended on a single crop like bananas.

### United Fruit Company

By 1930, the United Fruit Company controlled around 1.4 million hectares of land in Costa Rica, Guatemala, Honduras, and Panama, plus over 2,400 kilometers of railways, customs, telecommunications, and other essential services.

### Economic Control

In 1947 alone, U.S. business accounted for:
- 38% of GDP in Honduras
- 22.7% in Guatemala
- 16.5% in Costa Rica
- 12.3% in Panama

These countries became economic vassals—suppliers of raw materials and dumping grounds for U.S.-made goods.

> *"Exploited and looted by the United States, these countries have become its economic vassals."*

## Entry 5: The Trump/Donroe Doctrine (2025–2026)

### The Revival

In November 2025, the Trump administration's National Security Strategy explicitly stated:

> *"After years of neglect, the United States will reassert and enforce the Monroe Doctrine to restore American preeminence in the Western Hemisphere."*

### The "Donroe Doctrine"

Analysts have dubbed this revival the **"Donroe Doctrine"**—a reference to Trump's aggressive reinterpretation. Unlike the original defensive posture, the new doctrine moves toward an offensive assertion of U.S. power for military security, economic benefit, and expelling foreign influence.

### Key Actions

| Action | Date | Significance |
|--------|------|--------------|
| **Venezuela raid** | January 3, 2026 | U.S. forces captured President Maduro; justified as "law enforcement" |
| **Greenland threats** | January 2026 | Trump called anything short of U.S. control "unacceptable" |
| **Cuba ultimatum** | January 2026 | "Make a deal or face the consequences" |
| **Mexico/Colombia threats** | January 2026 | Threatened action against cartels |

### Beyond the Hemisphere

The "Donroe Doctrine" no longer stops at regional boundaries. It is about reshaping global energy and trade patterns. Under this expanded doctrine, Iran's energy wealth becomes more than a national asset—it becomes a lever in sustaining U.S. dominance.

> *"Two centuries later, the Monroe Doctrine has outgrown geography. It has become global."*

## Entry 6: Latin American Resistance

### Criticism from Regional Leaders

| Leader | Statement |
|--------|-----------|
| **Lula da Silva (Brazil)** | Condemned U.S. actions in Venezuela as "unacceptable," saying they violated sovereignty and set a dangerous precedent |
| **AMLO (Mexico)** | Refused to attend Summit of the Americas if Cuba, Nicaragua, and Venezuela were excluded |
| **Latin American experts** | Viewed U.S. action against Venezuela as an "upgraded version" of the Monroe Doctrine |

### The Core Critique

Critics have long pointed out that the doctrine carried no internal restraint on U.S. behavior—it condemned the European empire while mimicking its methods.

> *"By declaring America an 'exclusive sphere of influence,' the doctrine denies the freedom of Latin American states to establish relations with other powers, which constitutes a form of neocolonialism incompatible with contemporary international law."*

### The Historical Record

As Uruguayan writer Eduardo Galeano revealed in *Open Veins of Latin America*, this pattern of "externalized development" remains a recurring script: the underground wealth of other nations must fuel the U.S. economy.

> *"The history of U.S. interventionism in Latin America is a history of human rights violations, economic robbery, poverty and even genocide."*

### A Changing Region

Latin America no longer accepts being treated as a backyard. One Brazilian expert noted: "Washington insists on seeing the region as its exclusive sphere of influence. But Latin America no longer accepts being treated as a backyard."
