# Part 6: The War on Drugs as Monroe Doctrine — Plan Colombia, Mexico, and the Militarization of Counter-Narcotics

---

## 6.1 Context — From Cold War to Drug War

With the end of the Cold War (1989–1991), the ideological justification for U.S. intervention in Latin America — anti-communism — lost its central rationale. But the Monroe Doctrine did not disappear. Instead, it was rebranded. The "War on Drugs" became the new framework for U.S. military and political intervention in the hemisphere.

**The shift was seamless**:

| Era | Justification | Mechanism |
|-----|---------------|-----------|
| **Monroe Doctrine (1823)** | Hemispheric security | Rhetorical claim |
| **Roosevelt Corollary (1904)** | "Civilization" / stability | Gunboat diplomacy |
| **Cold War (1947–1991)** | Anti-communism | Covert action; proxy warfare |
| **Post-Cold War (1991–present)** | **War on Drugs** | **Militarized counter-narcotics; security assistance** |

**The logic**: The U.S. would combat drug trafficking in the hemisphere — not just as a law enforcement issue, but as a **national security** issue. This framing justified U.S. military presence, training, and operations across Latin America. The War on Drugs became the 21st-century version of the Roosevelt Corollary — the U.S. acting as the hemispheric policeman, now in the name of narcotics interdiction rather than anti-communism.

**Key drivers**:

| Driver | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **U.S. domestic politics** | The War on Drugs was popular in the U.S.; it was used to justify military spending and foreign interventions |
| **Geopolitical continuity** | The War on Drugs allowed the U.S. to maintain its military presence and influence in Latin America after the Cold War |
| **Economic interests** | U.S. corporations and financial institutions benefited from the neoliberal reforms that accompanied drug war policies |
| **Latin American dynamics** | Drug trafficking networks (e.g., Colombian cartels, Mexican cartels) were real threats to governance and stability |

## 6.2 The History of U.S. Drug Policy in Latin America

| Period | Policy | Key Features |
|--------|--------|--------------|
| **1910s–1940s** | Early drug control | U.S. pressured Latin American countries to criminalize drugs; limited enforcement |
| **1960s–1970s** | Nixon's War on Drugs | U.S. began providing law enforcement training and equipment to Latin American countries |
| **1980s** | Reagan's drug war escalation | Militarization of drug enforcement; support for paramilitary groups (e.g., Contras) engaged in drug trafficking |
| **1990s** | Plan Colombia | Massive U.S. military aid to Colombia; aerial fumigation; paramilitary ties |
| **2000s** | Mérida Initiative | U.S. security assistance to Mexico; militarization of Mexican drug enforcement |
| **2020s** | Continuation | Ongoing U.S. military aid, training, and intelligence support |

## 6.3 Plan Colombia (2000–2020s) — The Blueprint

**Plan Colombia** was a U.S.-funded military and counter-narcotics initiative in Colombia, launched in 2000. It was the largest U.S. foreign assistance program in the hemisphere.

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Context** | Colombia was in a decades-long civil conflict involving leftist guerrillas (FARC, ELN), right-wing paramilitaries (AUC), and drug cartels. Coca cultivation and cocaine trafficking funded all sides |
| **U.S. involvement** | Over $10 billion in U.S. aid (military, police, and economic); U.S. military advisors; aerial fumigation of coca fields; training and equipment for Colombian security forces |
| **Official justification** | Combating drug trafficking; strengthening democracy; reducing violence |
| **Outcome** | FARC was significantly weakened (peace accord signed in 2016); coca cultivation shifted to other areas; violence decreased in some regions, but paramilitary violence and human rights abuses continued |
| **Legacy** | Plan Colombia became the model for U.S. counter-narcotics assistance in other countries (e.g., Mexico, Central America). It demonstrated the militarization of the War on Drugs — the use of military force to combat drug trafficking, with significant human rights costs |

**The colonial echo**: Plan Colombia was a 21st-century version of the Roosevelt Corollary — the U.S. acting as the hemispheric policeman, intervening in a sovereign state's internal affairs to address a perceived threat. The justification was drug trafficking, but the mechanism was the same: U.S. military aid, training, and presence.

## 6.4 The Mérida Initiative (2008–Present) — Mexico and Central America

The **Mérida Initiative** is a U.S. security assistance program for Mexico and Central America, launched in 2008. It followed the Plan Colombia model but was tailored to the Mexican context.

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Context** | Mexico had been in a drug war since President Felipe Calderón's administration (2006); cartel violence was escalating; the U.S. feared a "failed state" on its southern border |
| **U.S. involvement** | Over $3 billion in U.S. aid (equipment, training, intelligence); U.S. military and intelligence advisors; cooperation with Mexican security forces |
| **Official justification** | Combating drug trafficking; strengthening the rule of law; reducing violence |
| **Outcome** | Violence continued; cartels fragmented and expanded; human rights abuses by Mexican security forces increased; coca cultivation shifted to other areas; the U.S. expanded support to other Central American countries |
| **Legacy** | The Mérida Initiative expanded the War on Drugs across the region; it militarized drug enforcement in Central America and deepened U.S. involvement in the region |

**The colonial echo**: The Mérida Initiative was an extension of the same logic — the U.S. using military assistance and training to combat drug trafficking, with significant sovereignty concerns and human rights costs.

## 6.5 The CIA-Contra Cocaine Connection — A Dark Legacy

The U.S. had a long history of involvement in drug trafficking — often covertly. During the Contra War (1980s), the CIA supported Contra rebels in Nicaragua, who were involved in drug trafficking. This was documented in the 1980s and later in the **Kerry Committee Report** (1989).

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Context** | The CIA was funding and training Contra rebels to overthrow the Sandinista government in Nicaragua. The Contras were engaged in cocaine trafficking to fund their operations |
| **U.S. involvement** | The CIA was aware of Contra drug trafficking; it did not stop it; it may have facilitated it in some cases |
| **Outcome** | The Contras were defeated (despite U.S. support); cocaine trafficking continued; the CIA's role was documented in the Kerry Report and later investigations |
| **Legacy** | The CIA-Contra cocaine connection is a documented chapter in U.S. covert operations; it is a reminder of the hypocrisy of U.S. drug policy |

**The colonial echo**: The CIA-Contra cocaine connection was a 20th-century version of the British East India Company's opium trade — the use of narcotics for strategic purposes. The U.S. was complicit in drug trafficking to achieve its geopolitical objectives.

## 6.6 Alternative Perspectives — Balancing the Analysis

### Realist/National Security View
- Drug trafficking is a real threat to governance, security, and public health. The U.S. has legitimate interests in combating drug production and trafficking.
- U.S. assistance has strengthened the capacity of Latin American security forces to combat organized crime.
- The War on Drugs has contributed to the weakening of major cartels (e.g., Cali, Medellín; Sinaloa) and the reduction of violence in some regions.

### Human Rights/Sovereignty Critique
- The War on Drugs has militarized Latin American security forces, leading to human rights abuses, torture, and extrajudicial killings.
- U.S. assistance has often supported regimes that violate human rights (e.g., Colombia, Mexico).
- The War on Drugs has failed to reduce drug consumption in the U.S. or to weaken drug trafficking networks. It has caused immense suffering in Latin America.

### Latin American Resistance/Alternative Approaches
- Several Latin American countries (e.g., Uruguay, Argentina, Mexico) have begun to decriminalize or legalize drugs, challenging the U.S. prohibitionist framework.
- The U.S. has pressured Latin American countries to maintain prohibitionist policies, limiting their policy autonomy.

### Economic/Neoliberal Angle
- The War on Drugs has been linked to neoliberal reforms (e.g., privatization, deregulation) that have benefited U.S. corporations.
- Drug trafficking networks have been used by economic elites to launder money and maintain power.

## 6.7 The Colonial Echo — From Monroe to Plan Colombia

| Era | Mechanism | Colonial Echo |
|-----|-----------|---------------|
| **Monroe Doctrine (1823)** | Rhetorical claim | European colonialism excluded |
| **Roosevelt Corollary (1904)** | Gunboat diplomacy | British gunboat diplomacy in China |
| **Good Neighbor (1933)** | Rhetorical non-intervention; economic control | Informal empire; corporate dominance |
| **Covert Empire (1947–1991)** | CIA regime change; state terror | British East India Company; colonial intelligence networks |
| **Cold War Direct Action (1961–1989)** | Overt military intervention | Gunboat diplomacy; colonial "police actions" |
| **War on Drugs (1991–present)** | **Militarized counter-narcotics; security assistance (blends military, economic, and diplomatic tools — hybrid of previous eras)** | **Resource extraction; dependency; corporate control** |

**The continuity**: The War on Drugs is the 21st-century version of the Roosevelt Corollary — the U.S. acting as the hemispheric policeman, using military force to address a perceived threat. The justification has changed (drugs instead of anti-communism), but the logic remains the same: the U.S. will intervene unilaterally in the hemisphere whenever it deems necessary.
