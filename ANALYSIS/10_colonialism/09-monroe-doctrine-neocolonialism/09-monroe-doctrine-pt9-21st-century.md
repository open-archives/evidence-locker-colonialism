# Part 9: 21st Century Monroe — The Americas in the U.S.-China Cold War

---

## 9.1 The Central Question Restated

The Monroe Doctrine, declared in 1823, established the Western Hemisphere as a U.S. sphere of influence. For two centuries, it has been the foundation of U.S. policy in the Americas — enforced through gunboat diplomacy, covert action, economic coercion, and military intervention. Its core premise was simple: no extra-hemispheric power would be allowed to establish a foothold in the Americas.

**The arrival of China as a global power has fundamentally challenged this premise.**

China is not a European colonial power, not the Soviet Union. It does not seek to establish military bases or formal colonies. Instead, it offers something more insidious from the U.S. perspective: **genuine economic alternatives.** Trade, investment, infrastructure, and critical minerals partnerships that Latin American countries find attractive — often more attractive than what the U.S. offers.

**The question at the heart of this concluding synthesis**: Is the Monroe Doctrine dead? Or has it simply been transformed — from a unilateral U.S. assertion of control to a contested framework in a multipolar world?

## 9.3 The "New Monroe Doctrine" — U.S. Responses to China

The Trump administration has explicitly revived the Monroe Doctrine as a framework for countering China. The 2025 U.S. National Security Strategy declares that "after years of neglect, the United States will reassert and enforce the Monroe Doctrine to restore American preeminence in the Western Hemisphere."

**The key elements of the "new" Monroe Doctrine**:

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Economic coercion** | Threats of tariffs, sanctions, and trade restrictions on countries that deepen ties with China |
| **Military presence** | Expanding military alliances, security assistance, and basing |
| **Diplomatic pressure** | Leveraging trade agreements (USMCA), regional institutions (OAS), and bilateral relationships |
| **Critical minerals competition** | U.S. efforts to secure supply chains and reduce dependence on Chinese processing |
| **Narrative framing** | Framing China as a "new colonialism" and the U.S. as the defender of freedom |

**The limits**: The U.S. has not stopped China's advancement. Tariffs and threats have motivated Latin America to diversify away from Washington. As one analysis notes: "The Monroe Doctrine treats cooperation as a threat and still thinks a tweet can reshape trade."

---

## 9.4 The "New Spheres" — A Multipolar Hemisphere

The Western Hemisphere is no longer a unipolar U.S. sphere. It is a contested space with multiple actors:

| Actor | Role | Mechanisms |
|-------|------|------------|
| **United States** | Traditional hegemon | Military, economic, diplomatic, cultural influence |
| **China** | Rising challenger | Trade, investment, infrastructure, critical minerals, BRI |
| **Russia** | Niche player | Security cooperation, military sales, political alignment |
| **Latin American States** | Increasingly autonomous | Diversification, regional integration, resource nationalism |
| **Regional Institutions** | CELAC, Mercosur, Pacific Alliance | Multilateral alternatives to U.S.-dominated OAS |

**The shift**: Latin American countries are not choosing between the U.S. and China. They are diversifying — building relationships with multiple powers to maximize their own leverage. This is a profound departure from the Cold War, when countries were forced to choose sides.

**The transformation**: The Monroe Doctrine is not dead. It is being transformed from a unilateral U.S. assertion of control to a contested framework in a multipolar world. The U.S. no longer has a monopoly on influence in the hemisphere, but it remains the dominant military and economic power. China is the challenger, not the replacement.

## 9.6 Alternative Perspectives — The Future of the Monroe Doctrine

### Realist Perspective
- The Monroe Doctrine remains strategically vital. China's presence in Latin America is a threat to U.S. security, especially in areas like critical minerals, technology, and military basing.
- The U.S. must compete aggressively — through trade, investment, and military presence — to maintain its influence.
- The doctrine is not dead; it is being tested and must be adapted.

### Liberal/Institutionalist Perspective
- The Monroe Doctrine is outdated. The U.S. should embrace multilateralism, working through the OAS and other regional institutions to build consensus.
- Coercion and unilateralism are counterproductive. The U.S. should offer positive alternatives — trade, investment, and development — rather than threats.
- The doctrine should be replaced by a partnership framework that respects Latin American sovereignty and agency.

### Latin American Perspective
- The Monroe Doctrine was never legitimate. It was an assertion of U.S. hegemony that violated Latin American sovereignty.
- China offers a genuine alternative — economic opportunity without the strings of U.S. interventionism.
- Latin American countries should chart their own course, building relationships with multiple powers to maximize their own interests.

### Critical/Post-Colonial Perspective
- The Monroe Doctrine and its replacements — neoliberalism, the War on Drugs, and now the "new" Monroe Doctrine — are all forms of imperial control.
- China's presence is not liberation; it is a new form of extractive relationship. Latin America remains a source of resources and a market for manufactured goods.
- True liberation requires breaking the cycle of dependency — not just switching from U.S. to Chinese domination.

## 9.7 The Road Ahead — Scenarios for 2030

| Scenario | Description | Likelihood |
|----------|-------------|------------|
| **A. U.S. Resurgence** | The U.S. successfully pushes China out through aggressive diplomacy, trade agreements, and military pressure | Low |
| **B. China Dominance** | China overtakes the U.S. as the primary economic partner in most Latin American countries; the U.S. retains military influence | Moderate |
| **C. Multipolar Equilibrium** | Latin American countries successfully balance U.S. and Chinese influence, maintaining autonomy and diversified relationships | Highest |
| **D. Conflict** | U.S.-China tensions escalate into a regional conflict (e.g., Venezuela, Taiwan proxy) | Low but rising |
| **E. Latin American Rejection** | Latin American countries reject both U.S. and Chinese domination, pursuing regional integration and independent development | Low |

**The most likely outcome**: A multipolar equilibrium in which Latin American countries maintain relationships with both the U.S. and China, leveraging their competition to maximize their own interests. The Monroe Doctrine will persist — but as a contested framework, not a unilateral assertion.

## 9.12 Final Verdict — The Monroe Doctrine in 2026

The Monroe Doctrine is **not dead**. It has been the foundation of U.S. policy in the hemisphere for two centuries, and it remains a central framework for U.S. thinking about Latin America. The Trump administration's explicit revival of the doctrine is evidence of its persistence.

**However, the Monroe Doctrine has been fundamentally transformed.** It is no longer a unilateral U.S. assertion of control. It is now a contested framework in a multipolar world. China's economic presence has created genuine alternatives for Latin American countries, and they are using that leverage to pursue their own interests.

**The irony**: The Monroe Doctrine was originally a warning to European powers to stay out of the Americas. Now it is a warning to China — but the U.S. no longer has the power to enforce it unilaterally. The Western Hemisphere is no longer a U.S. "backyard." It is a theater of great-power competition, where Latin American countries are increasingly the actors, not the objects.

**The final question**: Will the U.S. adapt to this new reality — offering positive alternatives, respecting Latin American sovereignty, and building genuine partnerships? Or will it cling to the old logic of unilateral control, treating cooperation as a threat and alienating its neighbors?

The answer to that question will determine the future of the Monroe Doctrine — and the future of the Americas.
