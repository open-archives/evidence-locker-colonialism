# Part 8: China in Latin America — The Monroe Doctrine Under Pressure

---

## 8.1 Context — The New Player in the Hemisphere

For nearly two centuries, the Monroe Doctrine served as the foundation of U.S. policy in the Western Hemisphere — a unilateral assertion that the Americas were a U.S. sphere of influence, closed to extra-hemispheric powers. First directed at European colonialism, then at Soviet communism, the doctrine was enforced through gunboat diplomacy, covert action, and economic coercion.

**The arrival of China has fundamentally altered this calculus.**

Since the early 2000s, China has emerged as a major economic power in Latin America. Trade has grown from roughly $12 billion in 2000 to over $518 billion in 2024 — an increase of more than 40-fold. By 2025, bilateral trade reached a record $549 billion. Chinese foreign direct investment (FDI) in the region rebounded from $8.7 billion in 2023 to $14.7 billion in 2024. Chinese firms have signed more than $300 billion in construction contracts across the region. China's accumulated investment stock in Latin America is estimated at roughly $650 billion, closing the gap with the U.S. total of approximately $1 trillion.

**The scale of China's engagement** has transformed Latin America from a U.S. "backyard" into a contested space — a theater of great-power competition where the Monroe Doctrine is being tested as never before.

**The regional shift**: In South America, China has already overtaken the United States as the primary economic partner in key countries including Chile, Peru, and Uruguay. Brazil sends about 28 percent of its exports to China, compared with roughly 13 percent to the United States. While the U.S.-Latin America trade figure ($1.2 trillion) remains higher, about two-thirds of that consists of commerce with Mexico alone.

### 8.2.2 Investment — The Infrastructure Push

Chinese investment in Latin America is concentrated in sectors that shape long-term development: energy, mining, infrastructure, and renewable energy. Key infrastructure projects include:

| Project | Country | Investment | Status |
|---------|---------|------------|--------|
| **Chancay Port** | Peru | $3.5 billion (total); $1.3 billion (first stage) | ~70% complete; expected launch November 2026 |
| **San Juan de Marcona Port** | Peru | $405 million | Construction began 2026 |
| **Bogotá Metro Line 1** | Colombia | (Chinese-built) | Expected to reduce commute times from 2+ hours to 27 minutes |
| **BYD EV plant** | Brazil | Former Ford plant in Bahia | Expected to produce 150,000 vehicles annually; create ~10,000 jobs |

**The Chancay Port project** is particularly significant. Completed in 2024, it reduced shipping times from Peru to Asia by about 12 days and generated more than $200 million in additional tax revenue in its first year. It has drawn interest from Brazil as a gateway for exports to Asia but has raised concerns in the United States and Europe. Chinese company Cosco Shipping holds a 60% share in the port.

**Job creation**: Chinese projects in Latin America have contributed to the creation of nearly one million jobs, while also supporting hospitals, schools, bridges, airports, roads, and ports across the region.

## 8.3 Critical Minerals — The New Extraction Frontier

Latin America's vast mineral wealth has made it central to China's resource security strategy. China is both the world's largest metals producer and consumer, yet its domestic reserves are limited:

| Mineral | China's Global Reserve Share | Key Import Sources (Latin America) |
|---------|------------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| **Copper** | 4.2% of global reserves | Chile (922,000 tons, 2024), Peru (699,000 tons) — combined 57.7% of Chinese imports |
| **Lithium** | Domestic supply meets ~1/3 of refining needs | Chile (78% of imports), Argentina (20%) — combined 98% of Chinese carbonate lithium imports |
| **Rare earths** | — | Brazil is world's second-largest rare earth reserve holder |

**The "Lithium Triangle"**: Bolivia, Argentina, and Chile together control over 50% of global lithium resources. Chinese companies are actively pursuing lithium projects in Argentina and Bolivia, building a more integrated EV supply chain across South America. Chinese company Lanshen has secured a 30% stake in a lithium project in Argentina.

**The EV ecosystem**: Chinese manufacturers are building Latin America's first fully functional EV ecosystem. Brazil is emerging as "the regional anchor" for vehicle assembly, while Argentina and Bolivia develop upstream segments of the supply chain. CATL holds 37% of the global battery market, and BYD sells more new energy vehicles than any other firm worldwide.

**Resource nationalism risks**: Some Latin American countries have pushed back. Chile launched a "National Lithium Strategy" in 2023 giving state copper company Codelco majority control. Mexico nationalized lithium in 2022. Bolivia imposes strict limits on foreign ownership.

## 8.4 The Diplomatic Dimension — Strategic Partnerships and BRI Expansion

### 8.4.1 Belt and Road Initiative in Latin America

Latin American states began joining China's Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) in 2017. As of January 2026, **22 Latin American countries** have signed BRI cooperation documents. The BRI has evolved from an Asia-centered infrastructure project to a global development strategy, with Latin America emerging as a key region of focus.

China has established **comprehensive strategic partnership agreements** with Peru, Brazil, Mexico, and Chile. The China-CELAC Forum (China-Community of Latin American and Caribbean States) serves as the primary multilateral framework for cooperation.

**Three pillars for the next phase** (announced at the 12th China-CELAC Infrastructure Cooperation Forum, June 2026):

| Pillar | Focus |
|--------|-------|
| **Infrastructure + Industrial Development** | Expanding Latin American participation in global production chains |
| **Ecological Transition** | Clean energy, green infrastructure, sustainable transportation, green finance |
| **Digitalization** | Moving beyond physical connectivity toward digital connectivity |

### 8.4.2 De-dollarization and Financial Channels

China is expanding financial cooperation in the region. In July 2026, the China Construction Bank issued a **2.7 billion yuan ($370 million) loan** to Brazil's state oil company Petrobras — the company's first yuan-denominated financing of the year. This reflects China's broader push to internationalize the yuan and reduce dollar dependence in Latin American trade.

Colombia has applied to join the China-led New Development Bank, which has approved loans for 122 infrastructure projects totaling more than $40 billion.

## 8.5 The U.S. Response — The Return of the Monroe Doctrine

The Trump administration has made the Monroe Doctrine a centerpiece of its Latin America policy. The **2025 U.S. National Security Strategy** declares that "after years of neglect, the United States will reassert and enforce the Monroe Doctrine to restore American preeminence in the Western Hemisphere". The doctrine has been updated — what some analysts call the **"Trump Corollary"** or **"Donroe Doctrine"** — with China as the primary target.

**Key U.S. actions**:

| Action | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Venezuela intervention** | Military operation capturing President Maduro, framed as rolling back Chinese/Russian influence |
| **Tariffs and sanctions** | Threatening tariffs, visa bans, and sanctions on countries that deepen ties with China |
| **Trade agreements** | Pushing Free Trade Agreements with Central American economies |
| **Military alliances** | Proposing Peru as a "major non-NATO ally" (joining Colombia, Argentina, Brazil) |
| **Security assistance** | Expanding security cooperation and military basing |

**The limits of U.S. pressure**: Despite the administration's aggressive posture, the U.S. has not stopped China's advancement in Latin America. Tariffs and interventionism have motivated Latin America to continue diversifying away from Washington. As one analysis puts it: "The Monroe Doctrine treats cooperation as a threat and still thinks a tweet can reshape trade". The Trump administration's objective of expelling China from Latin America "without providing a feasible alternative, can prove wrong". *(Note: While U.S. responses have varied tactically across different administrations, the strategic intensity of countering China in the region has consistently escalated in recent years.)*

## 8.6 Alternative Perspectives — Balancing the Analysis

### Latin American Agency — Not Just a Battlefield

Latin American countries are not passive objects in the U.S.-China competition. They actively shape their relationships with both powers:

| Country | Approach |
|---------|----------|
| **Brazil** | Lula da Silva has called for Mercosur to pursue a free trade agreement with China, while maintaining ties with the U.S. |
| **Mexico** | Tied to the U.S. through USMCA; increased tariffs on Chinese goods to appease Washington |
| **El Salvador** | Bukele government secured U.S. appeasement by allowing deportation of Venezuelan migrants |
| **Honduras** | 26% of GDP depends on U.S. remittances; vulnerable to U.S. pressure |

As one scholar notes, "the Chinese presence in Latin America relates to its gargantuan infrastructure investments," and threatening tariffs "without offering economic projects at Chinese rates does not in any way enhance the US image".

### The Developmental Angle

Proponents argue that China offers what the U.S. has not: long-term investment, infrastructure, and market access without Washington Consensus-style conditionalities. Chinese companies have demonstrated the ability to tailor products to local conditions while pursuing commercially driven partnerships. For Global South policymakers, "the cost of disengaging far outweighs setting clear, locally tailored partnership terms".

### The Critical Angle

Critics — including some Latin American governments and civil society — raise concerns about:

- **Debt dependency**: Chinese lending declined sharply after 2015, but concerns persist about opaque loan terms
- **Environmental impact**: Resource extraction in ecologically sensitive areas
- **Labor standards**: Concerns about working conditions in Chinese-operated projects
- **Resource nationalism**: Growing pushback from countries like Chile and Mexico seeking to retain control over strategic minerals

## 8.7 The Colonial Continuum — From Monroe to BRI

| Era | Mechanism | Colonial Echo |
|-----|-----------|---------------|
| **Monroe Doctrine (1823)** | Rhetorical claim | European colonialism excluded |
| **Roosevelt Corollary (1904)** | Gunboat diplomacy | British gunboat diplomacy in China |
| **Good Neighbor (1933)** | Rhetorical non-intervention; economic control | Informal empire; corporate dominance |
| **Covert Empire (1947–1991)** | CIA regime change; state terror | British East India Company; colonial intelligence networks |
| **War on Drugs (1991–present)** | Militarized counter-narcotics | Resource extraction; dependency |
| **Neoliberalism (1980s–present)** | Economic coercion; debt as control | Colonial indemnities; debt peonage |
| **China in Latin America (2000s–present)** | **Trade dependency; infrastructure; resource extraction** | **A new extractive relationship — but with multiple bidders** |

**The continuity and the rupture**: China's presence in Latin America represents both a continuation of the extractive relationship that has defined the hemisphere for centuries — resources flowing out, infrastructure flowing in — and a rupture in the monopoly the U.S. once held over that relationship. For the first time since the Monroe Doctrine was declared, Latin American countries have a genuine alternative to U.S. economic and political dominance.
