## 6.1 What Is the Digital Silk Road (DSR)?

The **Digital Silk Road (DSR)** is the technological backbone of the Belt and Road Initiative. Formally introduced by China in 2015, it encompasses a sprawling portfolio of digital infrastructure projects: undersea and terrestrial fiber-optic cables, 5G telecommunications networks, satellite navigation systems (BeiDou), cloud computing data centers, artificial intelligence (AI) surveillance systems, and e-commerce platforms.

**Scale (as of 2026)** : China has invested heavily in digital connectivity across Asia, Africa, the Middle East, Europe, and Latin America. The DSR is not a single project but a distributed architecture of digital control—a "nervous system" for the physical BRI infrastructure of ports, railways, and pipelines.

**Chinese framing**: The DSR bridges the "digital divide," bringing internet access, 5G connectivity, and e-commerce to developing countries that Western firms have neglected. It is about "inclusive globalization" and digital development.

**Critical framing**: The DSR is the 21st-century equivalent of the telegraph cables the British Empire laid across the 19th-century world—but with vastly more intrusive capabilities. Where the telegraph carried dispatches, the DSR carries *everything*: personal data, financial transactions, government communications, military logistics, and biometric surveillance. The DSR grants China a digital architecture of control that no colonial power ever possessed.

## 6.2 The Telegraph Parallel — Imperial Control at the Speed of Light

In the 19th century, the British Empire understood that control of communications was control of the empire. The **Eastern Telegraph Company** (later Cable & Wireless) laid a global network of undersea cables that connected London to Bombay (1870), Singapore, Hong Kong, Australia, and the Americas.

| Colonial Telegraph (19th C) | Digital Silk Road (21st C) |
|-----------------------------|----------------------------|
| Cable landings in colonial ports (Aden, Singapore, Hong Kong) | Cable landings in BRI ports (Djibouti, Gwadar, Kyaukphyu) |
| Messages routed through London for "security" | Data routed through Chinese servers for "security" (and surveillance) |
| Enabled rapid military command and control | Enables real-time AI surveillance, drone coordination, and cyberwarfare |
| British firms held a monopoly on cable manufacturing and laying | Huawei, ZTE, and Chinese SOEs dominate BRI digital infrastructure contracts |
| Cables were protected by Royal Navy gunboats | Chinese contracts include "security clauses" and diplomatic protection |
| Intelligence gathering via cable tapping | Intelligence gathering via backdoors, data centers, and AI algorithms |
| "Civilizing mission" justification | "Digital inclusion" and "development" justification |

**The continuity**: Just as the British telegraph allowed London to govern India in near-real-time (instead of months by sailing ship), the DSR allows Beijing to monitor, analyze, and influence BRI countries in real-time. But where the telegraph carried text, the DSR carries *all data*—and AI processes it at scales the British Empire could never have imagined.

## 6.3 Huawei, ZTE, and 5G — The Contested Infrastructure

Huawei and ZTE are the flagship corporations of the DSR. They build 5G networks, supply telecommunications equipment, and deploy smart city technologies across the Global South.

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Huawei global market share (5G)** | ~30–35% of global 5G network equipment (2026); dominant in emerging markets |
| **ZTE market share** | ~10–12% globally; strong in Asia, Africa, Latin America |
| **Countries with Huawei 5G bans** | U.S., UK, Australia, Canada (partial), Japan, Taiwan, EU members (limited) |
| **Countries with Huawei 5G deployment** | Most of Asia, Africa, Middle East, Latin America, and parts of Europe (e.g., Hungary, Greece) |

**The security concern**: Western intelligence agencies (U.S., UK, Australia, Canada) have alleged that Huawei equipment contains backdoors enabling Chinese state surveillance, that its supply chain is vulnerable to coercion, and that its compliance with Chinese national security laws (which compel companies to assist state intelligence) makes it a de facto arm of the Chinese state.

**China's counter**: Huawei denies backdoor allegations, has offered "no-spy" agreements, and argues that Western bans are protectionist and politically motivated. Chinese officials frame the bans as "tech decoupling" and "Cold War mentality."

**The colonial echo**: The British Empire used its control of submarine cable landing rights to exclude competitors and maintain imperial communications monopoly. Today, the U.S. and its allies use security reviews and bans to exclude Chinese equipment—a modern form of digital protectionism. But in BRI countries with fewer alternatives, Huawei and ZTE are often the *only* viable suppliers, replicating the monopoly dynamics of colonial communications.

## 6.4 Undersea Cables — The Data Silk Road

Over 95% of intercontinental data travels through undersea fiber-optic cables. China has aggressively expanded its cable footprint through the DSR.

| Cable Project | Route | Chinese Role | Status (2026) |
|---------------|-------|--------------|---------------|
| **PEACE Cable** | Pakistan-East Africa-Mediterranean-Europe | China-backed; Huawei Marine construction; landing in Pakistan, Djibouti, Egypt | Operational; connects Pakistan to Europe via Africa |
| **SEA-ME-WE 5** | Southeast Asia-Middle East-Western Europe 5 | Chinese investment/participation | Operational (since 2016); China part of consortium |
| **SEA-ME-WE 6** | Southeast Asia-Middle East-Western Europe 6 | China part of consortium; expected 2025–2027 | Under construction |
| **South-East Asia-Japan Cable 2 (SJC2)** | Asia-Japan | China Unicom, China Mobile participation | Operational |
| **2Africa** | Africa-Europe-Asia | Chinese participation limited | Operational; more Western-led |

**Strategic concern**: Cable landing points (e.g., Djibouti, Gwadar, Kyaukphyu) are co-located with Chinese ports and bases. Data transiting these cables can be intercepted, routed through Chinese-controlled infrastructure, or subjected to Chinese surveillance laws.

**Data routing asymmetry**: Chinese-backed cables often route data through China, creating a situation where non-Chinese data traverses Chinese territory (cyberspace) by design. This is the digital equivalent of the colonial "free ports" that British merchants used to circumvent local tariffs—except the toll is data, not goods.

## 6.5 BeiDou Satellite System — GPS for the BRI

**BeiDou** is China's global satellite navigation system, completed in 2020, with 55 satellites in orbit. It is China's independent alternative to the U.S. Global Positioning System (GPS), Russia's GLONASS, and the European Galileo system.

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Satellites** | 55 (operational) — complete global coverage |
| **Accuracy** | Civilian: ~1.5 meters; Military/encrypted: <0.1 meters |
| **Coverage** | Global (fully operational since 2020) |
| **BRI integration** | BeiDou receivers installed in BRI infrastructure (ports, railways, pipelines); smartphones sold in BRI countries include BeiDou capability |
| **Strategic value** | Provides China with independent navigation and timing for military missiles, drones, and naval operations; reduces dependence on U.S. GPS (which can be degraded or shut off in conflict) |

**Colonial parallel**: The British Empire relied on celestial navigation (sextants, chronometers) and later on the U.S. Navy's global radio navigation systems. Control over navigation meant control over global shipping lanes. BeiDou gives China independent navigation control—a strategic emancipation that echoes the 19th-century British push for global surveying and charting to dominate maritime trade.

**Dual-use reality**: BeiDou provides civilian navigation for BRI railways and ports, but its military encrypted channel is controlled solely by the PLA. This is civil-military fusion in orbit.

## 6.6 AI Surveillance Exports — Smart Cities, Facial Recognition, and Social Credit

China has aggressively exported AI surveillance technologies to BRI countries. Chinese companies (Hikvision, Dahua, Huawei, ZTE) supply facial recognition cameras, smart city platforms, and AI-powered public safety systems to governments across Asia, Africa, and Latin America.

| Country/Region | Chinese Surveillance Technology | Status (2026) |
|----------------|--------------------------------|---------------|
| **Uzbekistan** | AI facial recognition cameras (Hikvision); smart city systems | Operational |
| **Kazakhstan** | Integrated AI surveillance networks for public security | Operational |
| **Ecuador** | Chinese-built surveillance system (2019) — later scaled back due to privacy concerns | Partially operational |
| **Pakistan** | "Safe City" projects in Islamabad, Lahore, Karachi — Huawei/ZTE supplied | Operational; linked to CPEC security |
| **DRC** | Smart city systems in Kinshasa | Under deployment |
| **Zimbabwe** | AI surveillance for elections and public order | Deployed |
| **Myanmar** | Surveillance systems for military junta (dual-use: civilian + military repression) | Active; controversial |
| **Ethiopia** | Facial recognition and surveillance for urban centers | Operational |

**The social credit export**: Analysts have raised concerns that China is exporting elements of its domestic "social credit" system—a framework for monitoring and scoring citizen behavior—to BRI countries through these surveillance platforms. While China has not formally exported the social credit *score* itself, the underlying infrastructure (surveillance cameras, AI analytics, data integration) enables recipient governments to build their own scoring systems.

**Colonial parallel**: Colonial powers used census data, identity papers, and pass laws to control indigenous populations. China's AI surveillance exports are the digital version: biometric identification, real-time tracking, and algorithmic social sorting. The technology is far more sophisticated, but the *purpose*—population control and governance—is historically continuous.

## 6.7 The Data Colonialism Frame

**Data colonialism** is the thesis that contemporary digital extraction replicates the structure of historical colonialism. Data is the new raw material; AI is the new refinery; and algorithms are the new colonial administrators.

| Colonial Extraction (19th C) | Data Colonialism (21st C) |
|------------------------------|---------------------------|
| Resources (rubber, minerals, opium) extracted from colonies | Data (personal, financial, biometric) extracted from BRI countries |
| Processed in metropolitan factories (London, Brussels) | Processed in Chinese data centers and AI models |
| Finished goods returned to colonies at high prices | AI insights and digital services returned at Chinese-determined prices |
| Local elites co-opted to facilitate extraction | Local governments and firms partner with Chinese tech companies |
| Limited local value retention | Data value retained in China; local populations receive infrastructure |

**China's unique role**: Unlike Western tech giants (Google, Meta, Amazon), Chinese tech firms are tightly integrated with the Chinese state. Data flowing to Chinese servers is subject to Chinese national security laws (including the **National Intelligence Law** and **Cybersecurity Law**), which compel companies to assist state intelligence activities. This is not a privacy concern in the Western sense—it is a **sovereignty concern**.

**Counter-argument**: Proponents of the DSR argue that China provides digital infrastructure to countries that Western firms ignore. Africa, for example, has leapfrogged into digital economies using Chinese-built networks. Data colonialism theory, critics argue, ignores the genuine developmental benefits of connectivity and the agency of recipient governments to negotiate terms.
