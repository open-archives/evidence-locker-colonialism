## Executive Summary

The Berlin Conference convened 14 European powers plus the United States from November 1884 to February 1885 to partition African territory among themselves. No African representatives attended. The conference established rules for colonial acquisition that accelerated the "Scramble for Africa" within decades. Borders drawn at the conference persist in modern African nation-states and continue to fuel ethnic conflict and political instability.

**Key Finding**: The General Act of the Berlin Conference formalized "effective occupation" requirements that transformed nominal territorial claims into administrative control, enabling rapid resource extraction.

## Part 3: Key Articles From The Berlin Act (1885)

| Article | Content | Modern Impact |
|---------|---------|---------------|
| **Article 1** | Free trade guaranteed in Congo Basin | Commercial exploitation precedent |
| **Article 2** | Freedom of navigation on Congo/Niger rivers | Infrastructure control mechanisms |
| **Article 6** | Signatories protect indigenous populations, suppress slavery | Used as justification for "civilizing missions" |
| **Article 34** | Must notify other powers before claiming new coast territory | Formalized claim competition |
| **Article 35** | "Effective occupation" required to legitimize claims | Accelerated military/administrative deployment |
| **Protocol** | Congo Free State recognized as King Leopold II's private domain | Personal extraction regime |
